# Quick Definition

Armstrong completely enumerates the subgroups of $A_4$ (order 12) and proves that $A_4$ has no subgroup of order 6, demonstrating that the converse of Lagrange's theorem fails.

# Core Definition

By Lagrange's theorem, subgroup orders of $A_4$ must divide 12. Armstrong identifies: three subgroups of order 2 ($\{\varepsilon, (12)(34)\}$, $\{\varepsilon, (13)(24)\}$, $\{\varepsilon, (14)(23)\}$), four subgroups of order 3 (each generated by a 3-cycle), one subgroup of order 4 ($\{\varepsilon, (12)(34), (13)(24), (14)(23)\}$), plus the trivial subgroup and $A_4$ itself (Armstrong, Ch. 11, pp. 65-66).

The subgroup of order 4 is unique because all other non-identity elements of $A_4$ are 3-cycles, which cannot belong to a group of order 4 since 3 does not divide 4 by Corollary (11.2).

Armstrong then proves that **no subgroup of order 6 exists** in $A_4$ through a counting argument on 3-cycles and an application of Lagrange's theorem.

# Prerequisites

- **Lagrange's theorem** — Constrains possible subgroup orders
- **Alternating group** — $A_4$ is the group under study

# Key Properties

1. Subgroup orders must be divisors of 12: namely 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12
2. All divisors except 6 are realised as subgroup orders
3. The order-4 subgroup (Klein four-group) is unique and is a union of conjugacy classes
4. The absence of order-6 subgroups shows the converse of Lagrange's theorem fails

# Construction / Recognition

## Proof that no order-6 subgroup exists (p. 66):
1. Suppose $H \le A_4$ with $|H| = 6$
2. 3-cycles in $H$ come in inverse pairs, so their count is even
3. Cannot have six 3-cycles (need room for identity), so at most four
4. Four 3-cycles plus identity and their products give more than 6 elements — contradiction
5. Two 3-cycles force $H$ to contain the order-4 subgroup, but 4 does not divide 6

# Context & Application

This analysis is Armstrong's primary example showing that the converse of Lagrange's theorem fails. It motivates Cauchy's theorem (Ch. 13) as a partial converse for prime divisors.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 66): The subgroups of order 3 are $\{\varepsilon, (123), (132)\}$, $\{\varepsilon, (124), (142)\}$, $\{\varepsilon, (134), (143)\}$, $\{\varepsilon, (234), (243)\}$.

**Example 2** (p. 66): The unique subgroup of order 4 is $V = \{\varepsilon, (12)(34), (13)(24), (14)(23)\}$.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming $A_4$ must have a subgroup of order 6 because 6 divides 12
  **Correction**: The converse of Lagrange's theorem is false; $A_4$ is the standard counterexample

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking the failure of the converse is rare or pathological
  **Clarification**: While the converse fails for $A_4$, Cauchy's theorem and the Sylow theorems provide important partial converses
