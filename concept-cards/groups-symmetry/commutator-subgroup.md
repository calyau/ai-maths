# Quick Definition

The commutator subgroup $[G, G]$ is the subgroup of $G$ generated by all commutators $xyx^{-1}y^{-1}$. It is always a normal subgroup, and $G/[G,G]$ is the largest abelian quotient of $G$.

# Core Definition

The subgroup generated by all commutators is the **commutator subgroup** $[G, G]$ of $G$ (Armstrong, Ch. 15, p. 90).

**(15.6) Theorem.** The commutator subgroup is a normal subgroup, the quotient group $G/[G,G]$ is abelian, and if $H$ is a normal subgroup of $G$ for which $G/H$ is abelian, then $[G,G] \subseteq H$ (Armstrong, p. 90).

# Prerequisites

- **Commutator** â€” $[G,G]$ is generated by commutators
- **Normal subgroup** â€” $[G,G]$ is proved to be normal

# Key Properties

1. $[G,G] \triangleleft G$ (any conjugate of a commutator is a commutator)
2. $G/[G,G]$ is abelian (because $[G,G]xy = [G,G]yx$)
3. $[G,G]$ is the smallest normal subgroup with abelian quotient: if $G/H$ is abelian, then $[G,G] \subseteq H$
4. $[G,G] = \{e\}$ iff $G$ is abelian
5. Elements of $[G,G]$ are products of commutators (not necessarily single commutators)

# Construction / Recognition

## To Compute $[G,G]$:
1. Compute all commutators $xyx^{-1}y^{-1}$ for $x, y \in G$
2. Take the subgroup generated by these elements
3. This subgroup is automatically normal

# Context & Application

The commutator subgroup provides a systematic way to "abelianise" a group. The quotient $G/[G,G]$ is the abelianisation â€” the largest abelian group that $G$ can map onto. Armstrong uses the universal property (part 3 of Theorem 15.6) to characterise $[G,G]$ uniquely.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 90): $[S_n, S_n] = A_n$ for $n \ge 3$. Every commutator in $S_n$ is an even permutation, so $[S_n, S_n] \subseteq A_n$. Conversely, every 3-cycle is a commutator: $(abc) = (ab)(ac)(ab)(ac)$, and 3-cycles generate $A_n$.

**Example 2** (p. 91): $[D_n, D_n] = \langle r^2 \rangle$. Since $rsr^{-1}s^{-1} = r^2$, we have $r^2 \in [D_n, D_n]$. The quotient $D_n/\langle r^2 \rangle$ is abelian, so $[D_n, D_n] = \langle r^2 \rangle$. For odd $n$: $D_n/[D_n, D_n] \cong \mathbb{Z}_2$. For even $n$: $D_n/[D_n, D_n] \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$.

**Example 3** (p. 91): $[Q, Q] = \{\pm 1\}$. Since $Q/\{\pm 1\} \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$ is abelian, $[Q, Q] \subseteq \{\pm 1\}$, and $[Q, Q] = \{\pm 1\}$ because $Q$ is non-abelian.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming every element of $[G,G]$ is a single commutator
  **Correction**: Elements of $[G,G]$ are products of commutators; a product of commutators need not itself be a commutator

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking $[G,G] = G$ means $G$ is abelian
  **Clarification**: $[G,G] = G$ means $G$ is "perfect" â€” it equals its own commutator subgroup. $G$ is abelian iff $[G,G] = \{e\}$.
