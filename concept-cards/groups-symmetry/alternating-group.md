# Quick Definition

The alternating group $A_n$ is the subgroup of $S_n$ consisting of all even permutations. It has order $n!/2$.

# Core Definition

"(6.4) Theorem. The even permutations in $S_n$ form a subgroup of order $n!/2$ called the **alternating group** $A_n$ of degree $n$" (Armstrong, p. 37). The proof verifies closure (product of even permutations is even), inverses (inverse of an even permutation is even), and identity ($\varepsilon = (12)(12)$ is even). The order is $n!/2$ because the map $\alpha \mapsto (12)\alpha$ pairs even permutations with odd ones.

# Prerequisites

- **Symmetric group** — $A_n$ is a subgroup of $S_n$
- **Even permutation** — $A_n$ consists of exactly the even permutations

# Key Properties

1. $A_n$ has order $n!/2$
2. $A_n$ is a subgroup of $S_n$ (index 2)
3. For $n \ge 3$, $A_n$ is generated by the 3-cycles (Theorem 6.5)
4. $A_n$ is non-abelian for $n \ge 4$
5. $A_4$ has 12 elements; $A_5$ has 60 elements
6. The 3-cycle generation uses $(1a)(1b) = (1ba)$ to combine transpositions into 3-cycles (p. 37)

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct $A_n$:
1. List all elements of $S_n$
2. For each, determine its sign (even or odd)
3. Keep only the even permutations

## To Check Membership in $A_n$:
1. Write the permutation in disjoint cycle form
2. Count the number of even-length cycles
3. If this count is even, the permutation belongs to $A_n$

# Context & Application

The alternating group plays a central role in the study of symmetry. The rotational symmetry group of the regular tetrahedron is isomorphic to $A_4$, and the rotational symmetry group of the regular dodecahedron (and icosahedron) is isomorphic to $A_5$. In Galois theory, the non-simplicity of $A_4$ and the simplicity of $A_5$ explain why polynomials of degree $\le 4$ are solvable by radicals while the general quintic is not.

# Examples

**Example** (p. 37): The twelve elements of $A_4$:
- Identity: $\varepsilon$
- Products of two transpositions: $(12)(34)$, $(13)(24)$, $(14)(23)$
- 3-cycles: $(123)$, $(124)$, $(134)$, $(234)$, $(132)$, $(142)$, $(143)$, $(243)$

**Example** (p. 37): Writing $(13)(24)$ as a product of 3-cycles: $(13)(24) = (13)(12)(14)(12) = (123)(124)$.

## Related
- **Sign of a permutation** — $A_n = \ker(\operatorname{sgn})$

## Contrasts With
- **Symmetric group** — $S_n$ contains both even and odd permutations; $A_n$ has half the elements

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Forgetting that $A_n$ is generated by 3-cycles, not all cycles.
  **Correction**: Theorem 6.5 states that 3-cycles generate $A_n$ for $n \ge 3$, using the identity $(1a)(1b) = (1ba)$.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking $A_n$ is always abelian because its elements are "even."
  **Clarification**: "Even" refers to the sign, not commutativity. $A_n$ is non-abelian for $n \ge 4$.
