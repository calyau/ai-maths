# Quick Definition

A free group F(X) is a group in which every non-identity element can be expressed in a unique way as a reduced word in the alphabet X. Free groups have no relations among their generators beyond the group axioms.

# Core Definition

"If G has a free set of generators then it is a free group" (Armstrong, Ch. 27, p. 173). The free group F(X) generated by a set X is constructed as the group of reduced words in the alphabet X, with multiplication defined by concatenation followed by reduction. "This group of reduced words formed from the alphabet X is called the free group generated by (the elements of) X and will be denoted by F(X)" (Armstrong, p. 174).

# Prerequisites

- **Group** -- A free group is a special type of group
- **Reduced word** -- Elements of a free group are reduced words

# Key Properties

1. Every non-identity element has a unique expression as a reduced word in the generators
2. There are no relations among the generators (the generators are "free")
3. A free group on one generator is infinite cyclic (isomorphic to Z)
4. A free group on two or more generators is non-abelian (Armstrong, p. 175)
5. Every element of F(X) with |X| >= 2 has infinite order (Armstrong, p. 175)
6. F_n denotes "the" free group on n generators (unique up to isomorphism)
7. Abelianizing F_n gives Z^n (Theorem 27.2)
8. F_m is isomorphic to F_n if and only if m = n (Theorem 27.3)

# Construction / Recognition

## Construction of F(X)
1. Start with a set X (the alphabet/generators)
2. Form all words in X: finite products x_1^{m_1} x_2^{m_2} ... x_s^{m_s}
3. Define reduction: collect adjacent equal generators, omit zero powers
4. The set of all reduced words forms the group F(X)
5. Multiplication: concatenate two reduced words, then reduce
6. Identity: the empty word
7. Inverse of x_1^{n_1} ... x_k^{n_k} is x_k^{-n_k} ... x_1^{-n_1}

## Universal Property (Theorem 27.4)
X is a free set of generators for G if and only if every function f: X -> H (for any group H) extends uniquely to a homomorphism G -> H.

# Context & Application

Free groups are the "most general" groups on a given set of generators -- they impose no relations. Every group is a quotient of a free group (Armstrong, p. 176), which is the foundation for presentations. Armstrong develops free groups as the algebraic machinery needed for the Nielsen-Schreier theorem in Chapter 28.

**Structural significance:** The rank n of F_n is an isomorphism invariant, proved via abelianization (Theorem 27.3).

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 175): F_1 is infinite cyclic; the reduced words are the powers x^n for n in Z.

**Example 2** (p. 175): F_2 on generators x, y is non-abelian; xy and yx are distinct reduced words.

**Example 3** (Exercise 27.10, p. 178): F_2 contains subgroups isomorphic to F_n for every n.

**Example 4** (Exercise 27.13, p. 178): Z * Z (free product of Z with Z) is isomorphic to F_2.

- **Presentation of a group** -- Every group is F(X)/N for some normal subgroup N

## Contrasts With
- **Finitely presented group** -- Has defining relations; a free group has none

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming free groups are always non-abelian
  **Correction**: F_1 = Z is abelian; only free groups of rank >= 2 are non-abelian

- **Error**: Thinking elements of a free group can have finite order
  **Correction**: In a free group of rank >= 2, every non-identity element has infinite order

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Confusing a free group with a free abelian group
  **Clarification**: A free abelian group (like Z^n) allows reordering of generators; a free group does not -- xy and yx are different in F_2 but equal in Z^2

- **Confusion**: Thinking "free" means the group has no structure
  **Clarification**: Free groups have rich structure (subgroup theory, word problem); "free" means specifically that there are no relations among the generators
