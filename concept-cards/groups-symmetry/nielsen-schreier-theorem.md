# Quick Definition

The Nielsen-Schreier theorem states that every subgroup of a free group is free. Armstrong proves this using group actions on trees: a subgroup of a free group acts freely on the Cayley tree, so it is free.

# Core Definition

"(28.4) Nielsen-Schreier Theorem. Every subgroup of a free group is free" (Armstrong, Ch. 28, p. 186). The proof is remarkably short: "Let F be the free group generated by the set X and let G be a subgroup of F. We know that F acts freely on the tree Gamma(F, X) and consequently so does G. Hence, G is free by (28.3)."

# Prerequisites

- **Free group** -- The ambient group is free
- **Free action on tree theorem** -- Theorem 28.3 provides the key implication
- **Cayley graph** -- The Cayley graph of a free group is a tree (Theorem 28.1)

# Key Properties

1. Every subgroup of a free group is free
2. The proof is three lines, combining Theorems 28.1 and 28.3
3. For F_n of rank n and a subgroup of finite index k, the subgroup has rank k(n-1) + 1 (Schreier index formula, not stated by Armstrong)
4. A subgroup of a free group on 2 generators can have any countable rank, including infinite rank (Exercise 27.9)
5. The theorem does not hold for free abelian groups (a subgroup of Z^n is free abelian, but this is a different statement)

# Construction / Recognition

## Proof (Armstrong, p. 186)
1. Let F = F(X) be free with free generators X
2. By Theorem 28.1, Gamma(F, X) is a tree
3. F acts freely on Gamma(F, X) by left multiplication
4. Any subgroup G of F also acts freely on Gamma(F, X) (vertex stabilizers in G are subgroups of those in F, which are trivial)
5. By Theorem 28.3, G is a free group

# Context & Application

The Nielsen-Schreier theorem is a landmark result in combinatorial group theory, originally proved by J. Nielsen (1921) for finitely generated subgroups and extended by O. Schreier (1927) to all subgroups. Armstrong follows J.-P. Serre's elegant geometric proof using group actions on trees, which replaces algebraic manipulation with geometric reasoning.

The theorem has a striking corollary: the free group F_2 on two generators contains free subgroups of every countable rank (Exercise 27.9-27.10).

# Examples

**Example 1** (Exercise 27.9, p. 178): In F_2 = F({x, y}), the subgroup generated by {xyx^{-1}, x^2yx^{-2}, x^3yx^{-3}, ...} is free on infinitely many generators.

**Example 2** (Exercise 27.10, p. 178): F_2 contains a subgroup isomorphic to F_n for every positive integer n.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming the rank of a subgroup of F_n is at most n
  **Correction**: Subgroups of F_2 can have any countable rank, including infinite rank

- **Error**: Trying to apply Nielsen-Schreier to free abelian groups
  **Correction**: The theorem is about free groups (non-abelian for rank >= 2); for free abelian groups, a different (and easier) result applies

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking the proof requires algebraic manipulation of words
  **Clarification**: Armstrong's proof (following Serre) is entirely geometric, using group actions on trees

- **Confusion**: Believing the theorem implies all groups are free
  **Clarification**: The theorem only applies to subgroups of free groups; quotient groups of free groups (which include all groups) need not be free
