# Quick Definition

There are exactly seventeen wallpaper groups up to isomorphism: p1, p2, pm, pg, p2mm, p2mg, p2gg, cm, c2mm, p4, p4mm, p4gm, p3, p3m1, p31m, p6, p6mm. Armstrong classifies them by examining each lattice type and determining which point groups and isometry combinations are possible.

# Core Definition

"There are seventeen different wallpaper groups" (Armstrong, Ch. 26, p. 162). The classification proceeds by examining each of the five lattice types and enumerating the compatible point groups and the ways each orthogonal transformation can be realized (as a reflection or a glide). Armstrong proves no two of the seventeen are isomorphic using Theorems 26.1-26.4.

# Key Properties

1. The complete list organized by lattice type:
   - Oblique: p1, p2
   - Rectangular: pm, pg, p2mm, p2mg, p2gg
   - Centred rectangular: cm, c2mm
   - Square: p4, p4mm, p4gm
   - Hexagonal: p3, p3m1, p31m, p6, p6mm
2. No two of these groups are isomorphic (Theorems 26.1-26.4)
3. Point groups of the seventeen groups range from trivial to D_6
4. The classification is exhaustive: every wallpaper group is isomorphic to one of these seventeen

## To Determine a Pattern's Wallpaper Group
1. Identify the lattice type from the translation subgroup
2. Determine the point group (rotation orders and reflections present)
3. Check which orthogonal transformations are realized as reflections vs. glides
4. Match to one of the seventeen groups

# Context & Application

The classification of wallpaper groups is a landmark result in geometric group theory. Armstrong notes that "the three-dimensional case, of interest to crystallographers, is more complicated. There are two hundred and nineteen isomorphism classes of crystallographic groups in three dimensions" (Ch. 26, p. 170). Figure 26.2 shows examples of patterns from several cultures realizing some of the seventeen groups.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 163): p1 is the simplest wallpaper group, generated by two independent translations. Its elements are (ma + nb, I).

**Example 2** (p. 163): p2 has point group {+/- I}, containing all translations and half-turns about points (m/2)a + (n/2)b.

**Example 3** (pp. 163-164): pm has reflections in horizontal mirrors through and between lattice points; pg replaces these reflections with glide reflections.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Determining the wallpaper group from the point group alone
  **Correction**: Multiple wallpaper groups can share the same point group (e.g., pm, pg, p2, cm all have point group Z_2). The lattice type and realization of orthogonal transformations matter

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking p3m1 and p31m are the same because they have the same point group D_3
  **Clarification**: They differ in how the reflections relate to the lattice: in p31m, every order-3 rotation can be written as a product of two reflections; in p3m1, some cannot (Theorem 26.4)

- **Confusion**: Confusing p4mm and p4gm
  **Clarification**: In p4mm, reflections realize all mirror directions; in p4gm, some mirror directions are realized only by glides (Theorem 26.3)
