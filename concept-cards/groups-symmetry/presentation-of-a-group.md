# Quick Definition

A presentation of a group G consists of a set X of generators and a collection R of defining relations (words that equal the identity), such that G is isomorphic to F(X)/N, where N is the smallest normal subgroup of F(X) containing R.

# Core Definition

"The group determined by the set X of generators and the collection R of defining relations is defined to be the quotient group F(X)/N, where N is the smallest normal subgroup of F(X) which contains R. If G is any group isomorphic to F(X)/N the pair X, R is called a presentation for G" (Armstrong, Ch. 27, p. 176). Every group is a quotient of some free group, since the natural homomorphism pi: F(X) -> G (sending each word to the corresponding product in G) is surjective.

# Prerequisites

- **Free group** -- The presentation is a quotient of a free group
- **Normal subgroup** -- N is the smallest normal subgroup containing R
- **Quotient group** -- G = F(X)/N
- **First Isomorphism Theorem** -- Used to establish G = F(X)/ker(pi)

# Key Properties

1. Every group has a presentation (it is a quotient of a free group)
2. The same group can have many different presentations (Example (vii))
3. The defining relations specify which words in F(X) become the identity in G
4. N is generated by R and all conjugates of elements of R
5. A group with a finite presentation {x_1,...,x_s | w_1,...,w_t} is finitely presented

# Construction / Recognition

## To Write a Presentation
1. Choose generators X for G
2. Find all "essential" relations among the generators (words that equal e in G)
3. Verify these relations generate all of ker(pi) as a normal subgroup
4. The presentation is {X | R}

## To Verify a Presentation
1. Show the quotient F(X)/N surjects onto G (the relations hold in G)
2. Show |F(X)/N| <= |G| (no extra elements)
3. Conclude F(X)/N = G

# Context & Application

Presentations provide a compact, algebraic way to specify groups. Armstrong motivates presentations through the dihedral group D_n = {r, s | r^n, s^2, (rs)^2} at the start of Chapter 27. The concept bridges combinatorial group theory with the geometric groups studied earlier.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 176): D_n = {x, y | x^n, y^2, (xy)^2}. Armstrong verifies this by showing |F(X)/M| = 2n.

**Example 2** (p. 176): Z = {x | ---} (no relations; the free group on one generator).

**Example 3** (p. 176): Z_n = {x | x^n}.

**Example 4** (p. 177): Q = {x, y | x^4, x^2y^{-2}, xyxy^{-1}} (the quaternion group).

**Example 5** (p. 177): Z x Z = {x, y | xyx^{-1}y^{-1}} (the commutativity relation).

**Example 6** (p. 177): Z_6 = {x | x^6} = {x, y | x^3, y^2, xyx^{-1}y^{-1}} (two presentations for the same group).

**Example 7** (p. 177): pg = {x, y | x^2y^{-2}} and p3m1 = {x, y | x^2, y^3, (xy^{-1}xy)^3} (wallpaper groups).

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Thinking a presentation uniquely determines the generators' images
  **Correction**: A presentation defines a group up to isomorphism, but does not specify a particular copy of the group

- **Error**: Forgetting that the normal subgroup N includes all conjugates of R, not just R itself
  **Correction**: N is the normal closure of R: the smallest normal subgroup containing R

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking two groups with the same number of generators and relations must be isomorphic
  **Clarification**: The specific relations matter; {x, y | x^2, y^3} and {x, y | x^3, y^2} give different groups

- **Confusion**: Believing the word problem (deciding if two words represent the same element) is always solvable
  **Clarification**: The word problem for finitely presented groups is undecidable in general, though Armstrong does not discuss this
