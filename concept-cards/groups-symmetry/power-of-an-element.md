# Quick Definition

For an element x of a group, x^m denotes the product of m copies of x (for positive m), x^0 = e (the identity), and x^{-n} denotes the product of n copies of x^{-1}. The laws x^m * x^n = x^{m+n} and (x^m)^n = x^{mn} hold for all integers m, n.

# Core Definition

"Write x^m for the product of m copies of the element x, and x^{-n} for the product of n copies of x^{-1}. Then x^m * x^n = x^{m+n} and (x^m)^n = x^{mn} for any two integers m and n, provided we interpret x^0 as the identity" (Armstrong, Ch. 4, p. 24).

# Prerequisites

- **Group** — Powers are defined for group elements
- **Identity Element** — x^0 = e
- **Inverse Element** — Negative powers use inverses

# Key Properties

1. x^m is the product of m copies of x (m > 0)
2. x^0 = e (the identity)
3. x^{-n} is the product of n copies of x^{-1}
4. x^m * x^n = x^{m+n} for all integers m, n
5. (x^m)^n = x^{mn} for all integers m, n
6. These laws make sense because of the general associative law

# Construction / Recognition

## To Compute x^m:
1. If m > 0: multiply x by itself m times
2. If m = 0: the result is e
3. If m < 0: multiply x^{-1} by itself |m| times

# Context & Application

Power notation is essential for describing elements of dihedral groups (r^a, r^a*s) and cyclic groups. The power laws are used constantly in algebraic manipulations within groups.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 22): In D_3, r^2 = rr, r^3 = e. Since r^3 = e, we have r^{-1} = r^2.

**Example 2** (p. 24): In D_n, r^n = e and s^2 = e. Higher powers reduce: r^{n+1} = r, etc.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming x^m * y^m = (xy)^m
  **Correction**: This holds only if x and y commute; in general, (xy)^m is not equal to x^m * y^m

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: In additive groups, confusing x^m notation with multiplication
  **Clarification**: In additive groups, "x^m" means mx (m copies of x added together); e.g., "4th power of 3" in Z means 3 + 3 + 3 + 3 = 12
