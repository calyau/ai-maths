# Quick Definition

A frieze group is the symmetry group of a pattern that repeats along an infinite strip, characterized by having an infinite cyclic translation subgroup (one independent translation direction). There are exactly seven frieze groups.

# Core Definition

"A pattern which repeats in a regular fashion along an infinite strip is called a frieze, and the symmetry group of such a pattern is characterised by the requirement that its translation subgroup be infinite cyclic" (Armstrong, Exercise 26.6, p. 170). Armstrong assumes the strip is {(x,y) in R^2 : -1 <= y <= 1} with smallest translation tau(x,y) = (x+1, y), and shows seven groups result.

# Prerequisites

- **Euclidean group** -- Frieze groups are subgroups of E_2
- **Wallpaper group** -- Frieze groups are the one-dimensional analogue

# Key Properties

1. Translation subgroup is infinite cyclic (generated by a single translation)
2. There are exactly seven frieze groups
3. The point group is trivial, Z_2, or the Klein four-group (Exercise 26.8)
4. The seven groups are generated by combinations of tau, a glide g, rotation f, and reflections

# Construction / Recognition

## The Seven Frieze Groups (Exercise 26.6)
1. Generated by tau alone
2. Generated by the glide g(x,y) = (x + 1/2, -y)
3. Generated by tau and the half-turn f(x,y) = (-x, -y)
4. Generated by tau and the vertical reflection q(x,y) = (-x, y)
5. Generated by tau and the horizontal reflection fq
6. Generated by tau, f, and q
7. Generated by g, f, and q

# Context & Application

Frieze patterns appear in decorative borders, architectural friezes, and textile edgings. They are the one-dimensional analogue of wallpaper patterns. Armstrong introduces them in the exercises of Chapter 26 and asks the reader to prove they are all distinct (Exercise 26.7).

# Examples

**Example 1** (Exercise 26.6): Group (i), generated by tau alone, is the simplest frieze group -- an infinite cyclic group of translations.

**Example 2** (Exercise 26.6): Group (ii), generated by a glide, is also infinite cyclic but not isomorphic to (i) as a "frieze group" because the equivalence requires preserving isometry types.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Thinking there are more than seven frieze groups by considering all subgroups of E_2 with cyclic translation subgroup
  **Correction**: Many such subgroups are isomorphic as frieze groups; there are exactly seven equivalence classes

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking groups (i) and (ii) in Exercise 26.6 are the same because both are infinite cyclic
  **Clarification**: As abstract groups they are isomorphic, but as frieze groups they differ because the isomorphism does not preserve isometry types
