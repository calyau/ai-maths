# Quick Definition

Every finitely generated abelian group is isomorphic to $\mathbb{Z}_{m_1} \times \mathbb{Z}_{m_2} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}_{m_k} \times \mathbb{Z}^s$ where $m_1 \mid m_2 \mid \cdots \mid m_k$. The integers $m_1, \ldots, m_k$ are the torsion coefficients and $s$ is the rank.

# Core Definition

**(21.1) Theorem.** Any finitely generated abelian group is isomorphic to a direct product of cyclic groups

$$\mathbb{Z}_{m_1} \times \mathbb{Z}_{m_2} \times \cdots \times \mathbb{Z}_{m_k} \times \mathbb{Z}^s$$

in which $m_i$ is a factor of $m_{i+1}$ for $1 \le i \le k - 1$ (Armstrong, p. 126).

The number $s$ is the **rank** and $m_1, \ldots, m_k$ are the **torsion coefficients**.

# Prerequisites

- **Finitely generated abelian group** -- the objects being classified
- **Direct product** -- the canonical form is a direct product

# Key Properties

1. The divisibility chain $m_1 \mid m_2 \mid \cdots \mid m_k$ is essential for uniqueness
2. The rank $s$ counts the number of infinite cyclic factors
3. The torsion coefficients determine the finite part
4. Two f.g. abelian groups are isomorphic iff they have the same rank and torsion coefficients (Theorem 21.4)

# Construction / Recognition

## Proof Sketch (pp. 127--128):
1. If generators $x_1, \ldots, x_r$ have only the trivial relation, then $G \cong \mathbb{Z}^r$
2. Otherwise, find the smallest positive exponent $m_1$ across all relations and all minimal sets of generators
3. Show $m_1$ divides all other exponents in that relation
4. Change generators so that the relation becomes $z_1^{m_1} = e$
5. Split: $G \cong \mathbb{Z}_{m_1} \times G_1$ where $G_1$ is generated by $r - 1$ generators
6. Repeat on $G_1$ to get $m_2$ with $m_1 \mid m_2$
7. The process terminates because the number of generators decreases

# Context & Application

This is the fundamental structure theorem for abelian groups in Armstrong's text. It provides a complete set of invariants (rank and torsion coefficients) that determine a finitely generated abelian group up to isomorphism.

# Examples

**Example (i)** (p. 127): $\mathbb{Z}_6 \times \mathbb{Z}_{10} \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_3 \times \mathbb{Z}_{10} \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_{30}$. The torsion coefficients are 2, 30.

**Example (ii)** (p. 127): An abelian group of order 12 is either $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$ or $\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_6$.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Writing $\mathbb{Z}_6 \times \mathbb{Z}_{10}$ as the canonical form.
  **Correction**: Since $6 \nmid 10$, this is not in canonical form. Decompose: $\mathbb{Z}_6 \times \mathbb{Z}_{10} \cong \mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_{30}$.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking the decomposition into cyclic groups of prime-power order is the same as this theorem.
  **Clarification**: Both decompositions exist and are equivalent. Armstrong uses the divisibility-chain form $m_1 \mid m_2 \mid \cdots \mid m_k$, not the prime-power form.
