# Quick Definition

A transposition is a 2-cycle — a permutation that swaps exactly two elements and fixes all others. Every permutation can be written as a product of transpositions.

# Core Definition

"A 2-cycle is usually referred to as a transposition" (Armstrong, p. 34). A transposition $(ab)$ interchanges $a$ and $b$ while leaving all other elements fixed. Transpositions are the fundamental building blocks of permutations: Theorem 6.1 states that "the transpositions in $S_n$ together generate $S_n$" (p. 35).

# Prerequisites

- **k-cycle** — A transposition is a $k$-cycle with $k = 2$

# Key Properties

1. A transposition swaps exactly two elements and fixes all others
2. A transposition is its own inverse: $(ab)(ab) = \varepsilon$
3. Every transposition has order 2
4. The sign of any transposition is $-1$ (p. 36)
5. Every element of $S_n$ can be written as a product of transpositions (Theorem 6.1)
6. The transpositions $(12), (13), \ldots, (1n)$ generate $S_n$ (Theorem 6.2a)
7. The adjacent transpositions $(12), (23), \ldots, (n{-}1\,n)$ generate $S_n$ (Theorem 6.2b)
8. The identity $(ab) = (1a)(1b)(1a)$ allows generation from transpositions involving 1 (p. 35)

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct:
1. Choose two distinct elements $a, b \in \{1, \ldots, n\}$
2. Write $(ab)$: this permutation swaps $a$ and $b$, fixing everything else

## To Recognize:
1. Write the permutation in disjoint cycle form
2. It is a transposition if it consists of a single cycle of length 2

# Context & Application

Transpositions are the simplest non-identity permutations and serve as generators for $S_n$. The decomposition of a permutation into transpositions is not unique, but the parity (even or odd number of transpositions) is always well-defined, which is the basis for defining even/odd permutations and the alternating group.

# Examples

**Example** (p. 34): $(12)$, $(13)$, $(23)$ are the three transpositions in $S_3$.

**Example** (p. 35): Any cyclic permutation can be written as a product of transpositions:
$$(a_1 a_2 \ldots a_k) = (a_1 a_k) \ldots (a_1 a_3)(a_1 a_2).$$

**Example** (p. 35): $(15)(246) = (15)(26)(24)$, decomposing into three transpositions (odd permutation).

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming the decomposition into transpositions is unique.
  **Correction**: The decomposition is not unique, but the parity of the number of transpositions is always the same.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Confusing "disjoint transpositions" with "product of transpositions."
  **Clarification**: Disjoint cycle decomposition uses disjoint cycles, but writing a permutation as a product of transpositions generally requires non-disjoint transpositions.
