# Quick Definition

The point group J of a subgroup G of E_2 is the image pi(G) under the projection pi: E_2 -> O_2 defined by pi(v, M) = M. It is a finite subgroup of O_2 measuring the rotational and reflective symmetry of a wallpaper pattern.

# Core Definition

"If G is a subgroup of E_2, we write ... J for pi(G), calling ... J the point group of G" (Armstrong, Ch. 25, p. 153). Here pi: E_2 -> O_2 is the homomorphism pi(v, M) = M. Since H = ker(pi|_G), the First Isomorphism Theorem gives J isomorphic to G/H. The point group acts on the lattice (Theorem 25.2) and must satisfy the crystallographic restriction (Theorem 25.3).

# Prerequisites

- **Wallpaper group** -- The point group is one of the two key invariants
- **Orthogonal group** -- J is a subgroup of O_2
- **Quotient group** -- J is isomorphic to G/H

# Key Properties

1. J = pi(G) is a subgroup of O_2
2. J is isomorphic to G/H where H is the translation subgroup
3. J is finite (by definition of wallpaper group)
4. J acts on the lattice L (Theorem 25.2)
5. J is either cyclic or dihedral (since all finite subgroups of O_2 are)
6. Rotation orders in J are restricted to 1, 2, 3, 4, 6 (crystallographic restriction)
7. If two wallpaper groups are isomorphic, their point groups are isomorphic (Corollary 25.6)

# Construction / Recognition

## To Compute the Point Group
1. Given a wallpaper group G with generators
2. For each generator (v, M), extract the matrix M
3. The point group J is generated by these matrices
4. J is a finite subgroup of O_2

# Context & Application

The point group is central to the wallpaper classification. It constrains which lattice types are compatible with a given wallpaper group and determines the basic symmetry structure. Armstrong uses the point group as the primary organizing principle for the classification in Chapter 26.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 153): The unshaded hexagonal pattern has point group D_6, generated by rotation through pi/3 and reflection in the x-axis.

**Example 2** (p. 153): After shading, the point group reduces to Z_2, generated by reflection in the x-axis.

**Example 3** (Ch. 26): The wallpaper groups p4mm and p4gm both have point group D_4, yet they are not isomorphic.

# Relationships

## Enables
- **Crystallographic restriction** -- Constrains the rotation orders in J

## Related
- **Translation subgroup** -- H is the kernel of pi restricted to G; J = G/H

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming the point group is always a subgroup of G itself
  **Correction**: J is a subgroup of O_2, not necessarily a subgroup of G. There may be no copy of J inside G (Armstrong p. 155)

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking isomorphic point groups imply isomorphic wallpaper groups
  **Clarification**: Multiple non-isomorphic wallpaper groups can have the same point group (e.g., pm, pg, p2 all have point group Z_2)
