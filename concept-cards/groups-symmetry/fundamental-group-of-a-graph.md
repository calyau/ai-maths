# Quick Definition

The fundamental group of a graph Gamma based at a vertex v is the group of equivalence classes of loops (closed paths) based at v, where two loops are equivalent if one can be obtained from the other by adding or removing round trips. It is always a free group.

# Core Definition

Armstrong defines a loop based at v as a path in Gamma from v to itself, and introduces an equivalence relation where two loops P and Q are equivalent if one can be transformed to the other by successively adding or removing single round trips. "The collection of all equivalence classes of loops based at v forms a group under the product [P][Q] = [PQ]" (Armstrong, Exercise 28.9, p. 186). Exercise 28.10 establishes that "the fundamental group of Gamma based at v is isomorphic to the free group F(X)" where X comes from edges not in a maximal tree.

# Key Properties

1. The fundamental group consists of equivalence classes of loops
2. Two loops are equivalent if related by adding/removing round trips
3. The group operation is concatenation of loops
4. For a tree, the fundamental group is trivial (every loop contains round trips only)
5. The fundamental group of a connected graph is free, generated by one edge per non-tree edge (Exercise 28.10)
6. The generators correspond to edges not in a maximal tree Lambda

# Construction / Recognition

## To Compute the Fundamental Group (Exercise 28.10)
1. Find a maximal tree Lambda in Gamma (contains all vertices)
2. For each edge not in Lambda, choose one representative from each pair {alpha, alpha-bar}
3. Let X be the resulting collection of edges
4. The fundamental group is isomorphic to F(X)

# Context & Application

The fundamental group of a graph connects combinatorial group theory to algebraic topology. Armstrong introduces it in the exercises as a way to see why graphs "naturally" produce free groups. The key result -- that the fundamental group of a graph is always free -- is a combinatorial analogue of a theorem in algebraic topology.

# Examples

**Example 1** (Exercise 28.8): For a tree, there is exactly one equivalence class of paths between any two vertices, so the fundamental group is trivial.

**Example 2** (Exercise 28.10): For a graph with one vertex and one pair of edges (a single loop), the fundamental group is Z = F_1.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming the fundamental group depends on the choice of basepoint v
  **Correction**: For connected graphs, the fundamental group is independent of the basepoint up to isomorphism

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Confusing the fundamental group of a graph with the fundamental group of a topological space
  **Clarification**: They are closely related -- a graph can be viewed as a 1-dimensional CW complex, and the two notions agree. But Armstrong's definition is purely combinatorial
