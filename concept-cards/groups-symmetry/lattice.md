# Quick Definition

A lattice L is the set of all integer linear combinations ma + nb of two independent vectors a and b. It is the orbit of the origin under the translation subgroup of a wallpaper group.

# Core Definition

Theorem 25.1 establishes: "The set L is the lattice spanned by a and b. That is to say, L consists of all linear combinations ma + nb where m, n in Z" (Armstrong, Ch. 25, p. 154). Here a is a non-zero vector of minimum length in L, and b is a vector skew to a of smallest possible length. The lattice L is the orbit of the origin under the action of the translation subgroup H on R^2.

# Prerequisites

- **Translation subgroup** -- The lattice is the orbit of the origin under H
- **Wallpaper group** -- Lattices arise from the translation subgroup of wallpaper groups

# Key Properties

1. L = {ma + nb : m, n in Z} for suitably chosen basis vectors a, b
2. L is a subgroup of the additive group R^2
3. a is chosen as a non-zero vector of minimum length in L
4. b is chosen skew to a with minimum length among such vectors
5. The point group J acts on L (Theorem 25.2)
6. L can be classified into exactly five types based on the shape of the basic parallelogram

# Construction / Recognition

## To Determine the Lattice
1. Find all translations in the wallpaper group G
2. Express these as vectors in R^2
3. Select a as a shortest non-zero vector
4. Select b as a shortest vector skew to a
5. Verify L = {ma + nb : m, n in Z}

# Context & Application

The lattice is the geometric foundation of the wallpaper classification. Its shape constrains which point groups are compatible: for example, a hexagonal lattice is required for 3-fold or 6-fold rotational symmetry, while a square lattice is needed for 4-fold symmetry. The proof of Theorem 25.1 uses a covering argument with parallelograms.

The three-dimensional analogue models atomic structure in crystals: "the corresponding lattice is spanned by three independent vectors and gives a configuration of points which models the internal atomic structure found in crystals" (Armstrong, p. 155).

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 153): The hexagonal pattern has lattice generated by translations tau_1 and tau_2.

**Example 2** (p. 155): The wallpaper group generated by tau(x,y) = (x+1,y) and h(x,y) = (-x, y+1) has lattice spanned by a = (1,0) and b = (0,2).

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Confusing the lattice with the translation subgroup
  **Correction**: The lattice L is a set of points in R^2; the translation subgroup H is a group of isometries. They are isomorphic as groups but are different mathematical objects

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking any two linearly independent vectors generate the same lattice
  **Clarification**: Different choices of basis can generate different lattices; the lattice is determined by the translation subgroup, not an arbitrary choice of basis
