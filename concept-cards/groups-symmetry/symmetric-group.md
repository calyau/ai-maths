# Quick Definition

The symmetric group $S_n$ is the group of all permutations of the first $n$ positive integers under composition. Its order is $n!$.

# Core Definition

"When $X$ consists of the first $n$ positive integers, then $S_X$ is written $S_n$ and called the **symmetric group** of degree $n$. The order of $S_n$ is $n!$" (Armstrong, p. 33). The group operation is composition of functions, with the identity permutation $\varepsilon$ as the identity element.

# Prerequisites

- **Permutation** — Elements of $S_n$ are permutations of $\{1, 2, \ldots, n\}$
- **Group axioms** — Must verify closure, associativity, identity, and inverses

# Key Properties

1. $S_n$ has order $n!$
2. $S_n$ is not abelian for $n \ge 3$
3. $S_n$ is generated by the transpositions $(12), (13), \ldots, (1n)$ (Theorem 6.2a)
4. $S_n$ is generated by the adjacent transpositions $(12), (23), \ldots, (n{-}1\,n)$ (Theorem 6.2b)
5. $S_n$ is generated by the transposition $(12)$ and the $n$-cycle $(12 \ldots n)$ (Theorem 6.3)
6. Every element of $S_n$ can be written as a product of transpositions (Theorem 6.1)

# Context & Application

The symmetric group is one of the most important groups in algebra. It serves as a "universal" group for finite groups via Cayley's theorem: every finite group of order $n$ is isomorphic to a subgroup of $S_n$. The symmetric groups also appear as symmetry groups of regular solids (e.g., the rotational symmetry group of the cube is isomorphic to $S_4$).

# Examples

**Example** (p. 33): The six elements of $S_3$ are $\varepsilon$, $(12)$, $(13)$, $(23)$, $(123)$, $(132)$.

**Example** (p. 33): The non-commutativity of $S_3$:
$$(12)(23) = (123), \quad \text{whereas} \quad (23)(12) = (132).$$

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Computing $\alpha\beta$ by applying $\alpha$ first.
  **Correction**: $\alpha\beta(x) = \alpha(\beta(x))$; apply $\beta$ first, then $\alpha$.

- **Error**: Assuming $S_n$ is abelian for small $n$.
  **Correction**: $S_n$ is abelian only for $n \le 2$. Already $S_3$ is non-abelian.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Confusing $S_n$ with $\mathbb{Z}_n$ (the cyclic group of order $n$).
  **Clarification**: $S_n$ has order $n!$ and is generally non-abelian; $\mathbb{Z}_n$ has order $n$ and is always abelian.
