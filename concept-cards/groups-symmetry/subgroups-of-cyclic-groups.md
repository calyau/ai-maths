# Quick Definition

Every subgroup of a cyclic group is itself cyclic. In particular, every subgroup of Z is of the form dZ = {..., -2d, -d, 0, d, 2d, ...} for some non-negative integer d, and every subgroup of Z_n is generated by some power of the generator.

# Core Definition

"**Theorem.** (a) Every subgroup of Z is cyclic. (b) Even better, every subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic" (Armstrong, Theorem 5.3, Ch. 5, p. 32).

# Prerequisites

- **Cyclic Group** — The theorem applies to cyclic groups
- **Subgroup** — The theorem characterizes subgroups
- **Additive Group of Integers** — Part (a) is about subgroups of Z

# Key Properties

1. Every subgroup of Z is of the form dZ for some non-negative integer d
2. Every subgroup of a cyclic group G = <x> is generated by some power x^d
3. The proof for Z uses the division algorithm
4. The proof for cyclic groups reduces to the Z case

# Construction / Recognition

## Proof of (a) - Subgroups of Z:
1. If H = {0}, H is cyclic (trivially)
2. If H != {0}, H contains a positive integer (since H contains inverses)
3. Let d be the smallest positive integer in H
4. For any n in H, divide: n = qd + m with 0 <= m < d
5. m = n - qd is in H (since n, qd are both in H)
6. Since d is minimal and m < d, we must have m = 0
7. So n = qd, meaning H = dZ = <d>

## Proof of (b) - Subgroups of Cyclic Groups:
1. Let G = <x> be cyclic, K a non-trivial subgroup
2. Every element of K is a power of x
3. Let H = {n in Z : x^n in K}; then H is a subgroup of Z
4. By (a), H = <d> for some d
5. Then K = <x^d>

# Context & Application

This theorem is fundamental for understanding the subgroup structure of cyclic groups completely. For Z_n, the subgroups correspond bijectively to the divisors of n: for each divisor d of n, there is exactly one subgroup of order d, generated by n/d.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 32): If H is a subgroup of Z and d is its smallest positive element, then H = dZ.

**Example 2** (p. 28): The subgroups of Z_6 are: {0} (generated by 0), {0, 3} (generated by 3), {0, 2, 4} (generated by 2), and Z_6 itself (generated by 1). Each corresponds to a divisor of 6.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming part (a) is obvious without proof
  **Correction**: The proof requires the division algorithm and the well-ordering of positive integers

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking subgroups of non-cyclic groups are always cyclic
  **Clarification**: The theorem applies ONLY to cyclic groups; non-cyclic groups can have non-cyclic subgroups
