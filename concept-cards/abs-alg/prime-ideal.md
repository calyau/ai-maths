# Quick Definition
A proper ideal P of a commutative ring R is prime if whenever $ab \in P$, then $a \in P$ or $b \in P$.

# Core Definition
Assume R is commutative. An ideal P is called a *prime ideal* if $P \neq R$ and whenever the product $ab$ of two elements $a, b \in R$ is an element of P, then at least one of $a$ and $b$ is an element of P (Dummit & Foote, p. 256).

# Prerequisites
- **Ideal** — A prime ideal is a special kind of ideal
- **Commutative ring** — The standard definition applies to commutative rings
- **Integral domain** — The quotient by a prime ideal is an integral domain

# Key Properties
1. P is prime iff $R/P$ is an integral domain (Proposition 13, p. 257)
2. Every maximal ideal is prime (Corollary 14, p. 258)
3. The converse is not true: $(x)$ is prime but not maximal in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ (p. 258)
4. In a PID, every nonzero prime ideal is maximal (Proposition 7, p. 283)
5. In $\mathbb{Z}$, the prime ideals are $(0)$ and $(p)$ for prime $p$ (p. 258)
6. Every prime ideal contains all nilpotent elements (Exercise 26, p. 260)

# Construction / Recognition
## To Recognize:
1. Verify P is a proper ideal ($P \neq R$)
2. Check: if $ab \in P$ then $a \in P$ or $b \in P$
3. Equivalently, check that $R/P$ is an integral domain

# Context & Application
Prime ideals generalize prime numbers from $\mathbb{Z}$ to arbitrary commutative rings. They are fundamental in algebraic geometry (the "points" of affine varieties correspond to prime ideals) and in algebraic number theory.

# Examples
**Example 1** (p. 258): In $\mathbb{Z}$, the prime ideals are $(0)$ and $(p)$ for prime $p$.

**Example 2** (p. 258): $(x)$ is a prime ideal in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ since $\mathbb{Z}[x]/(x) \cong \mathbb{Z}$, which is an integral domain.

**Example 3** (p. 258): $(x)$ is prime but not maximal in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, since $(x) \subset (2, x) \subset \mathbb{Z}[x]$.

# Relationships

## Enables
- **integral-domain** — R is an integral domain iff $(0)$ is a prime ideal

## Related
- **quotient-ring** — $R/P$ is an integral domain iff P is prime
- **maximal-ideal** — Every maximal ideal is prime

## Contrasts With
- **maximal-ideal** — A maximal ideal gives a quotient that is a *field*; a prime ideal gives only an integral domain

# Common Errors
- **Error**: Assuming every prime ideal is maximal
  **Correction**: $(0)$ in $\mathbb{Z}$ and $(x)$ in $\mathbb{Z}[x]$ are prime but not maximal

# Common Confusions
- **Confusion**: Confusing "prime ideal" with "ideal generated by a prime element"
  **Clarification**: In a PID these coincide, but in general a prime ideal need not be principal
