# Quick Definition
The tensor product $M \otimes_R N$ of a right R-module M and a left R-module N is an abelian group that universally linearizes R-balanced (bilinear) maps from $M \times N$.

# Core Definition
Let N be a left R-module and M a right R-module. The tensor product $M \otimes_R N$ is the quotient of the free abelian group on $M \times N$ by the subgroup generated by all elements $(m_1 + m_2, n) - (m_1, n) - (m_2, n)$, $(m, n_1 + n_2) - (m, n_1) - (m, n_2)$, and $(mr, n) - (m, rn)$. The coset of $(m, n)$ is denoted $m \otimes n$ (a simple tensor), and every element is a finite sum of simple tensors. The relations force $(m_1 + m_2) \otimes n = m_1 \otimes n + m_2 \otimes n$, $m \otimes (n_1 + n_2) = m \otimes n_1 + m \otimes n_2$, and $mr \otimes n = m \otimes rn$ (Dummit & Foote, pp. 369-370).

# Key Properties
1. **Universal property** (Theorem 10): R-balanced maps $M \times N \to L$ correspond bijectively to group homomorphisms $M \otimes_R N \to L$
2. $R \otimes_R N \cong N$ for any left R-module N
3. **Associativity**: $(M \otimes_R N) \otimes_T L \cong M \otimes_R (N \otimes_T L)$
4. **Commutativity** (for commutative R): $M \otimes_R N \cong N \otimes_R M$
5. **Distributivity over direct sums**: $(M \oplus M') \otimes_R N \cong (M \otimes_R N) \oplus (M' \otimes_R N)$
6. $R^s \otimes_R R^t \cong R^{st}$ for free modules over commutative R
7. Not every element is a simple tensor

# Construction / Recognition
## To Construct:
1. Form the free abelian group on $M \times N$
2. Quotient by the subgroup generated by the bilinearity and R-balanced relations
3. Denote the image of $(m, n)$ as $m \otimes n$

## To Compute in Practice:
1. Express elements as sums of simple tensors
2. Use the relations to simplify: $mr \otimes n = m \otimes rn$, linearity in each factor
3. Remember that representation as sum of simple tensors is not unique

# Context & Application
Tensor products are fundamental in algebra, geometry, and physics. They enable extension of scalars (changing the base ring), construct algebras, and play a central role in homological algebra. The tensor product functor is right exact but not left exact in general.

# Examples
**Example 1** (p. 376): $\mathbb{Z}/m\mathbb{Z} \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} \mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/d\mathbb{Z}$ where $d = \gcd(m, n)$.
**Example 2** (p. 376): $\mathbb{Q} \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} A = 0$ for any finite abelian group A.
**Example 3** (p. 376): $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} = 0$ but $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \cong \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$.

# Common Errors
- **Error**: Assuming every tensor is a simple tensor $m \otimes n$. **Correction**: General tensors are sums of simple tensors, and not every tensor can be written as a single simple tensor.
- **Error**: Assuming $m \otimes n = m' \otimes n'$ implies $m = m'$ and $n = n'$. **Correction**: The same tensor can have many different representations.

# Common Confusions
- **Confusion**: Thinking $M \otimes_R N$ always contains (a copy of) both M and N. **Clarification**: The map $n \mapsto 1 \otimes n$ need not be injective; e.g., $\mathbb{Q} \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} A = 0$ for finite A.
- **Confusion**: Believing tensor products are always nonzero. **Clarification**: $\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \otimes_{\mathbb{Z}} \mathbb{Z}/3\mathbb{Z} = 0$.
