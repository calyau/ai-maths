# Quick Definition
A nonzero element p in an integral domain R is prime if the ideal $(p)$ is a prime ideal, i.e., p is not a unit and whenever $p \mid ab$, then $p \mid a$ or $p \mid b$.

# Core Definition
The nonzero element $p \in R$ is called *prime* in R if the ideal $(p)$ generated by p is a prime ideal. Equivalently, p is a nonzero non-unit such that whenever $p \mid ab$ for any $a, b \in R$, then either $p \mid a$ or $p \mid b$ (Dummit & Foote, p. 284).

# Prerequisites
- **Integral domain** — Primes are defined in integral domains
- **Prime ideal** — A prime element generates a prime ideal

# Key Properties
1. Prime implies irreducible (Proposition 10, p. 285)
2. Irreducible does NOT always imply prime (p. 285)
3. In a PID, prime = irreducible (Proposition 11, p. 284)
4. In a UFD, prime = irreducible (Proposition 12, p. 286)
5. The prime elements of $\mathbb{Z}$ are $\pm p$ for prime numbers p

# Construction / Recognition
## To Verify:
1. Check p is nonzero and not a unit
2. Check: if $p \mid ab$ then $p \mid a$ or $p \mid b$

# Context & Application
Prime elements are crucial for unique factorization. The distinction between prime and irreducible is the key to understanding when unique factorization fails.

# Examples
**Example 1**: In $\mathbb{Z}$, the prime elements are $\pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 5, \ldots$

**Example 2** (p. 285): In $\mathbb{Z}[\sqrt{-5}]$, the element 3 is irreducible but NOT prime: $3 \mid (2+\sqrt{-5})(2-\sqrt{-5}) = 9$ but $3 \nmid (2+\sqrt{-5})$ and $3 \nmid (2-\sqrt{-5})$.

# Common Errors
- **Error**: Assuming irreducible elements are always prime
  **Correction**: This holds in PIDs and UFDs but fails in general integral domains

# Common Confusions
- **Confusion**: Confusing "prime element" with "prime number"
  **Clarification**: In $\mathbb{Z}$, these coincide (up to sign); in other rings, "prime" has the abstract meaning
