# Quick Definition
A polynomial is homogeneous (or a form) if all its monomial terms have the same total degree. Every polynomial can be decomposed uniquely into its homogeneous components.

# Core Definition
A polynomial is called *homogeneous* or a *form* if all its terms have the same degree. If f is a nonzero polynomial, the sum of all monomial terms of degree k is the *homogeneous component* of f of degree k. Any polynomial f of degree d can be written uniquely as $f = f_0 + f_1 + \cdots + f_d$ where $f_k$ is the homogeneous component of degree k (Dummit & Foote, p. 298).

# Prerequisites
- **Polynomial in several variables** — Homogeneity applies to multivariate polynomials

# Key Properties
1. The product of two homogeneous polynomials is homogeneous (Exercise 16, p. 299)
2. $p(\lambda x_1, \ldots, \lambda x_n) = \lambda^k p(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ for a homogeneous polynomial of degree k (Exercise 15)
3. An ideal is *homogeneous* iff it can be generated by homogeneous polynomials (Exercise 17)

# Examples
**Example** (p. 298): In $\mathbb{Z}[x,y]$, $f = -3x^2y^2 + 5xy^3 - 2y^4$ is homogeneous of degree 4.
