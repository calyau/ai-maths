# Quick Definition
The lattice of subgroups of a finite group G is a diagram showing all subgroups ordered by inclusion, with lines connecting subgroups that are directly contained in one another (with no subgroup between them).

# Core Definition
The *lattice of subgroups* of a finite group G is a directed graph with: all subgroups as vertices, 1 at the bottom, G at the top; subgroups of larger order higher; upward lines from A to B when $A \leq B$ with no subgroup strictly between them. The *join* of H and K is $\langle H, K \rangle$ (smallest subgroup containing both); the *meet* is $H \cap K$. Isomorphic groups have the same lattice, but the converse is false (Dummit & Foote, pp. 66-71).

# Prerequisites
- **Subgroup**, **Subgroup generated by a subset**

# Key Properties
1. $\{1\}$ at bottom, G at top
2. Join of H, K is $\langle H, K \rangle$; meet is $H \cap K$
3. Isomorphic groups have identical lattices (but non-isomorphic groups can too)
4. By the Lattice Isomorphism Theorem, the lattice of G/N appears as the "top part" of the lattice of G

# Context & Application
The lattice provides a visual summary of group structure, making it easier to identify subgroups, compute normalizers and centralizers, and understand quotient groups.

# Examples
**Example 1** (p. 67): The lattice of $Z_n$ corresponds to the divisor lattice of n.
**Example 2** (p. 68): The lattice of $D_8$ has 10 subgroups arranged in a characteristic pattern.
**Example 3** (p. 68): $Q_8$ and $D_8$ have different lattices despite both having order 8.
