# Quick Definition
A subgroup of a group G is a nonempty subset H that is closed under products and inverses, making H itself a group under the same operation. We write $H \leq G$.

# Core Definition
The subset H of G is a *subgroup* of G if H is nonempty and H is closed under products and inverses (i.e., $x, y \in H$ implies $x^{-1} \in H$ and $xy \in H$). The *Subgroup Criterion* (Proposition 1): H is a subgroup iff (1) $H \neq \emptyset$ and (2) for all $x, y \in H$, $xy^{-1} \in H$. For finite H, it suffices to check nonemptiness and closure under multiplication (Dummit & Foote, pp. 46-48).

# Prerequisites
- **Group** — subgroups are subsets of groups

# Key Properties
1. The identity of H equals the identity of G
2. Inverses in H equal inverses in G
3. Every group has trivial subgroups: $\{1\}$ and G itself
4. "Is a subgroup of" is transitive
5. The intersection of any collection of subgroups is a subgroup (Proposition 8)
6. For finite H: nonempty + closed under multiplication $\implies$ subgroup

# Construction / Recognition
## To Identify/Recognize:
1. Check $H \neq \emptyset$ (often: $1 \in H$)
2. Check $xy^{-1} \in H$ for all $x, y \in H$ (one-step subgroup test)
3. Alternatively for finite H: check closure under multiplication

# Context & Application
Subgroups are the primary tool for analyzing group structure. The lattice of subgroups of G encodes the internal structure of G. Key subgroups include cyclic subgroups, centralizers, normalizers, centers, and kernels.

# Examples
**Example 1** (p. 47): $\mathbb{Z} \leq \mathbb{Q} \leq \mathbb{R}$ under addition.
**Example 2** (p. 47): The rotations $\{1, r, r^2, \ldots, r^{n-1}\}$ form a subgroup of $D_{2n}$ of order n.
**Example 3** (p. 48): $\mathbb{Z}^+$ is NOT a subgroup of $\mathbb{Z}$ (no identity, no inverses).

# Common Errors
- **Error**: Checking closure under multiplication but not inverses for infinite subgroups. **Correction**: For infinite H, closure under multiplication alone does not suffice; must also check inverses.

# Common Confusions
- **Confusion**: Thinking any subset closed under the operation is a subgroup. **Clarification**: Must also contain the identity and be closed under inverses (automatic for finite subsets closed under the operation).
