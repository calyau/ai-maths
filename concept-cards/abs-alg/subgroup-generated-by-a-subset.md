# Quick Definition
For a subset A of G, $\langle A \rangle$ is the smallest subgroup containing A, equal to the intersection of all subgroups containing A. Its elements are all finite products of elements of A and their inverses.

# Core Definition
For any subset A of G, define $\langle A \rangle = \bigcap_{A \subseteq H \leq G} H$, the intersection of all subgroups of G containing A. This is a subgroup by Proposition 8. Equivalently, $\langle A \rangle = \{a_1^{\epsilon_1} a_2^{\epsilon_2} \cdots a_n^{\epsilon_n} \mid a_i \in A, \epsilon_i = \pm 1, n \geq 0\}$ (Proposition 9). When $A = \{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$ we write $\langle a_1, \ldots, a_n \rangle$ (Dummit & Foote, pp. 61-65).

# Prerequisites
- **Subgroup** — $\langle A \rangle$ is a subgroup

# Key Properties
1. $\langle A \rangle$ is the unique smallest subgroup containing A
2. Elements are finite products of elements of A and their inverses
3. For abelian G with finite $A = \{a_1, \ldots, a_k\}$: $\langle A \rangle = \{a_1^{\alpha_1} \cdots a_k^{\alpha_k} \mid \alpha_i \in \mathbb{Z}\}$
4. For non-abelian G, the structure can be much more complex
5. $|\langle A \rangle| \leq |a_1| \cdots |a_k|$ for abelian G with finite generator orders

# Context & Application
This construction generalizes cyclic subgroups ($A = \{x\}$ gives $\langle x \rangle$) and is used to describe subgroups by generators.

# Examples
**Example 1** (p. 64): $D_{2n} = \langle r, s \rangle$ and $S_n = \langle (12), (123\ldots n) \rangle$.
**Example 2** (p. 65): In $GL_2(\mathbb{R})$, two elements of order 2 can generate an infinite subgroup.
