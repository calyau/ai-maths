# Quick Definition
The nilradical $\mathfrak{N}(R)$ of a commutative ring R is the ideal consisting of all nilpotent elements of R.

# Core Definition
The set of nilpotent elements of a commutative ring R forms an ideal called the *nilradical* of R, denoted $\mathfrak{N}(R)$ (Exercise 29, Dummit & Foote, p. 251). The proof uses the Binomial Theorem to show closure under addition.

# Prerequisites
- **Commutative ring** — R must be commutative for the nilradical to be an ideal
- **Ideal** — The nilradical is an ideal
- **Nilpotent element** — The nilradical collects all nilpotent elements

# Key Properties
1. $\mathfrak{N}(R)$ is an ideal of R (Exercise 29, p. 251)
2. $\mathfrak{N}(R/\mathfrak{N}(R)) = 0$ (Exercise 30, p. 251)
3. $\mathfrak{N}(R)$ is contained in every prime ideal of R (Exercise 26, p. 260)
4. $\mathfrak{N}(R)$ equals the intersection of all prime ideals of R (Section 15.2)

# Construction / Recognition
## To Compute:
1. Find all elements $x \in R$ with $x^m = 0$ for some $m > 0$
2. In $\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z}$: the nilradical is generated by the product of distinct prime factors of n

# Context & Application
The nilradical measures the "non-reducedness" of a ring. Quotienting by the nilradical gives the largest quotient with no nilpotent elements.

# Examples
**Example 1**: $\mathfrak{N}(\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z}) = 6\mathbb{Z}/12\mathbb{Z} = \{\bar{0}, \bar{6}\}$ since $12 = 2^2 \cdot 3$ and $6 = 2 \cdot 3$.

**Example 2**: $\mathfrak{N}(\mathbb{Z}) = (0)$ since $\mathbb{Z}$ has no nonzero nilpotent elements.

# Relationships

## Related
- **prime-ideal** — The nilradical is contained in every prime ideal
- **radical-ideal** — Generalization: $\text{rad}(I) = \{r \mid r^n \in I\}$

# Common Errors
- **Error**: Assuming the nilradical is well-defined in noncommutative rings
  **Correction**: The set of nilpotent elements need not be an ideal in noncommutative rings

# Common Confusions
- **Confusion**: Confusing the nilradical with the Jacobson radical
  **Clarification**: The nilradical is the intersection of all prime ideals; the Jacobson radical is the intersection of all maximal ideals. Always $\mathfrak{N}(R) \subseteq \text{Jac}(R)$
