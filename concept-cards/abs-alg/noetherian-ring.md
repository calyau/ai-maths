# Quick Definition
A commutative ring R is Noetherian if it satisfies the ascending chain condition (A.C.C.) on ideals: every increasing chain of ideals eventually stabilizes.

# Core Definition
A commutative ring R is said to be **Noetherian** or to satisfy the ascending chain condition on ideals if there is no infinite increasing chain of ideals in R. Equivalently (Theorem 2, p. 669): (1) R is Noetherian, (2) every nonempty set of ideals of R contains a maximal element under inclusion, (3) every ideal of R is finitely generated. Every PID is Noetherian since all ideals are generated by a single element.

# Prerequisites
- **ring** — Noetherian is a property of commutative rings with identity
- **ideal** — The condition involves chains of ideals

# Key Properties
1. Every nonempty set of ideals contains a maximal element under inclusion
2. Every ideal is finitely generated
3. Quotients of Noetherian rings are Noetherian (Proposition 1)
4. A Noetherian ring may have arbitrarily long ascending chains and infinite descending chains
5. Every PID is Noetherian
6. The polynomial ring k[x_1,...,x_n] over a field k is Noetherian (Corollary 4)

# Construction / Recognition
## To Construct:
1. Start with a PID (automatically Noetherian)
2. Form polynomial rings over Noetherian rings (by Hilbert Basis Theorem)
3. Take quotients of Noetherian rings

## To Recognize:
1. Check if every ideal is finitely generated
2. Verify no infinite strictly ascending chain of ideals exists

# Context & Application
Noetherian rings are the natural generalization of PIDs and are central to commutative algebra and algebraic geometry. The Noetherian property of polynomial rings k[x_1,...,x_n] (via Hilbert's Basis Theorem) ensures that affine algebraic sets are defined by finitely many polynomial equations. This was one of the main original motivations for the concept (Dummit & Foote, p. 670).

# Examples
**Example** (p. 669): Z, k[x] for k a field, and the Gaussian integers Z[i] are all Noetherian since they are PIDs. The ring Z[x_1,x_2,...] of polynomials in infinitely many variables is not Noetherian since the ideal (x_1,x_2,...) is not finitely generated.

**Example** (p. 669): Z has the infinite descending chain (2) ⊃ (4) ⊃ (8) ⊃ ..., showing a Noetherian ring need not satisfy D.C.C.

# Common Errors
- **Error**: Assuming a Noetherian ring has no infinite descending chains of ideals. **Correction**: The A.C.C. only concerns ascending chains; Z has infinite descending chains.

# Common Confusions
- **Confusion**: Thinking "finitely generated as a ring" implies Noetherian. **Clarification**: Finitely generated k-algebras are Noetherian, but finitely generated rings in general need not be.
