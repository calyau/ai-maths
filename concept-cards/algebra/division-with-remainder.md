# Quick Definition

Division with remainder for polynomials states that given a monic polynomial f and any polynomial g with coefficients in a ring R, there exist unique polynomials q and r such that g = fq + r, where r = 0 or deg r < deg f.

# Core Definition

Let R be a ring, let f be a monic polynomial and let g be any polynomial, both with coefficients in R. There are uniquely determined polynomials q and r in R[x] such that g(x) = f(x)q(x) + r(x), and such that the remainder r, if not zero, has degree less than the degree of f. Moreover, f divides g in R[x] if and only if r = 0 (Artin, Proposition 11.2.9, p. 342).

# Prerequisites

- **Polynomial ring** -- Division occurs within R[x]
- **Degree of polynomial** -- Used to bound the remainder

# Key Properties

1. Requires f to be monic (or have unit leading coefficient)
2. Quotient q and remainder r are uniquely determined
3. f divides g if and only if r = 0
4. The remainder has strictly smaller degree than the divisor

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct:
1. Write g = f * (leading term match) + remainder
2. Repeat with the remainder until its degree is less than deg f

# Context & Application

Division with remainder is the foundation for the Euclidean algorithm in polynomial rings over fields, making F[x] a Euclidean domain.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 342): One cannot divide x^2 + 1 by 2x + 1 in Z[x] because the leading coefficient is not a unit, but one can in Q[x].

**Example 2** (p. 342, Corollary 11.2.11): The remainder of dividing g(x) by x - alpha is g(alpha). Thus x - alpha divides g if and only if g(alpha) = 0.
