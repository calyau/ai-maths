# Quick Definition

An ideal I of a ring R is a nonempty subset closed under addition and under multiplication by any element of R. Ideals are the ring-theoretic analogue of normal subgroups for groups.

# Core Definition

An ideal I of a ring R is a nonempty subset of R with these properties: (1) I is closed under addition, and (2) if s is in I and r is in R, then rs is in I (Artin, Definition 11.3.13, p. 347). Equivalently, a linear combination r_1 s_1 + ... + r_k s_k of elements s_i of I with coefficients r_i in R is in I.

# Prerequisites

- **Ring** -- Ideals are subsets of rings

# Key Properties

1. The kernel of any ring homomorphism is an ideal
2. I satisfies all subring requirements except I generally does not contain 1
3. The only ideal containing a unit is R itself (the unit ideal)
4. The zero ideal is (0) = {0}
5. The unit ideal is (1) = R

# Construction / Recognition

## To Recognize:
1. Check I is nonempty
2. Verify I is closed under addition
3. Verify that for any s in I and any r in R, the product rs is in I

# Context & Application

Ideals are the building blocks for quotient ring constructions. They play the same role for rings that normal subgroups play for groups: they are precisely the kernels of homomorphisms.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 347): The multiples of an element a form the principal ideal (a) = aR.

**Example 2** (p. 347): In R[x], the set of polynomials vanishing at 2 is the principal ideal (x - 2).

**Example 3** (p. 347): The ideal (p, x) in Z[x] generated by a prime p and x consists of polynomials with constant term divisible by p.

# Relationships

## Builds Upon
- **Ring** -- Ideals are special subsets of rings

## Enables
- **Quotient ring** -- R/I is a ring when I is an ideal
- **Prime ideal** -- Special ideals related to integral domains
- **Maximal ideal** -- Special ideals related to fields

## Contrasts With
- **Subring** -- A subring contains 1; an ideal generally does not (unless I = R)

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking ideals are subrings
  **Clarification**: An ideal I is not a subring unless I = R, because it typically does not contain 1
