# Quick Definition

A principal ideal is an ideal generated by a single element a, consisting of all multiples of a in the ring. It is denoted (a) = aR = {ra | r in R}.

# Core Definition

In any ring R, the multiples of a particular element a form an ideal called the principal ideal generated by a. An element b is in this ideal if and only if a divides b in R. The principal ideal is denoted (a) = aR = Ra = {ra | r in R} (Artin, formula 11.3.15, p. 348).

# Prerequisites

- **Ideal** -- A principal ideal is a special case of an ideal

# Key Properties

1. (1) = R is the unit ideal
2. (0) = {0} is the zero ideal
3. An ideal is proper if it is neither (0) nor (1)
4. a divides b if and only if b is in (a)
5. The unit ideal (1) is the only ideal containing a unit

# Context & Application

Many important rings have the property that every ideal is principal (PIDs). In Z and F[x], all ideals are principal.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 348): In Z, every ideal is of the form (n) = nZ for some non-negative integer n.

**Example 2** (p. 348): In F[x], every ideal is generated by the unique monic polynomial of lowest degree it contains (Proposition 11.3.22).
