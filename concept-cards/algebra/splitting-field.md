# Quick Definition

A splitting field for a polynomial f over F is the smallest extension field K in which f factors completely into linear factors. Any two splitting fields of f over F are isomorphic.

# Core Definition

A splitting field for a polynomial f over F is an extension field K/F such that (1) f splits completely in K, say f(x) = (x - alpha_1)...(x - alpha_n) with alpha_i in K, and (2) K is generated by the roots: K = F(alpha_1, ..., alpha_n) (Artin, p. 488, Section 16.3). Every polynomial has a splitting field (Lemma 16.3.1(b)), any two splitting fields are isomorphic (Proposition 16.4.3(b)), and a splitting field is a finite extension of F (16.3.1(c)).

# Prerequisites

- **Field extension** -- A splitting field is a specific type of extension
- **Irreducible polynomial** -- The polynomial being split

# Key Properties

1. Every polynomial has a splitting field (existence, 15.6.3 and 16.3.1(b))
2. Any two splitting fields of f over F are isomorphic extensions (16.4.3(b))
3. A splitting field is a finite extension (16.3.1(c))
4. Splitting Theorem: if K is a splitting field over F, then any irreducible polynomial over F with one root in K splits completely in K (Theorem 16.3.2)
5. A finite extension K/F is a Galois extension iff K is a splitting field over F (Theorem 16.6.4)

# Context & Application

Splitting fields are the extensions where Galois theory operates. The equivalence between "splitting field" and "Galois extension" (in characteristic zero) is one of the key results of Chapter 16. Splitting fields provide the natural setting for studying the roots of polynomials.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 489): The splitting field of x^3 - 2 over Q is Q(cube_root(2), omega), which has degree 6 over Q, where omega = e^{2pi*i/3}.

**Example 2** (p. 489): The splitting field of x^2 - 2 over Q is Q(sqrt(2)), which has degree 2.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking the splitting field depends on the particular polynomial used
  **Clarification**: Different polynomials can give the same splitting field. The key property is the Splitting Theorem (16.3.2).
