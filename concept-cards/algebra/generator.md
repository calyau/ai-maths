# Quick Definition

A subset U of a group G generates G if every element of G can be written as a product of elements of U and their inverses. A cyclic group is generated by a single element.

# Core Definition

A subset U of G generates G if every element of G is a product of a string of elements of U and their inverses (Artin, p. 59). The subgroup generated by U is the smallest subgroup containing U. For a cyclic group <x>, the single element x generates the group.

# Prerequisites

- **Group** — Generators produce group elements
- **Subgroup** — The subgroup generated by U is the smallest containing U

# Key Properties

1. G = <U> means every element is a product from U and U^(-1)
2. A cyclic group has a single generator
3. Elementary matrices generate GL_n (Theorem 1.2.16)
4. {x, y} generates S_3 (formula 2.2.7)

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct:
1. Choose elements of G; the subgroup they generate is the smallest containing them

## To Recognize:
1. Every element of G can be expressed using elements of U and their inverses

# Context & Application

Generators give compact descriptions of groups. S_3 needs only 2 generators (x and y) despite having 6 elements. Defining relations describe how generators interact.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 53): S_3 is generated by x = (123) and y = (12).

**Example 2** (p. 59): Elementary matrices generate GL_n.

# Relationships

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Forgetting that generators' inverses are also needed
  **Correction**: Products can include elements of U AND their inverses

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking generators must be independent
  **Clarification**: A generating set can be redundant; independence is a separate concept
