# Quick Definition

A lattice in the plane is the set of all integer linear combinations of two linearly independent vectors (a lattice basis). Every nonzero ideal in the ring of integers of an imaginary quadratic field is a lattice.

# Core Definition

A lattice L in the plane R^2 is generated by a lattice basis B = (v_1, v_2) consisting of two linearly independent vectors. Every element of L is an integer combination of the basis vectors in exactly one way (Artin, Section 13.10, p. 422). The area Delta(L) of the fundamental parallelogram is independent of the choice of basis (Corollary 13.10.7).

# Prerequisites

- **Ring of integers** -- Ideals in quadratic integer rings are lattices

# Key Properties

1. A lattice basis has two elements; every element is a unique integer combination
2. The area Delta(L) = Delta(R) * [R:L] is a lattice invariant
3. Sublattice L of M has finite index [M:L]
4. The shortest vector in a lattice satisfies |v|^2 <= (2/sqrt(3)) * Delta(L) (Lemma 13.10.8)

# Context & Application

Lattice theory underlies the geometry of algebraic integers in imaginary quadratic fields. Ideals are lattices, and the lattice structure controls ideal properties.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 396): The Gaussian integers Z[i] form a square lattice with basis (1, i).

**Example 2** (p. 396): The ring Z[(1+sqrt(-3))/2] forms an equilateral triangular lattice.
