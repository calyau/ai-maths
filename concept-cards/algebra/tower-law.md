# Quick Definition

The tower law states that for a chain of field extensions F in K in L, the degrees multiply: [L:F] = [L:K][K:F]. Both [L:K] and [K:F] divide [L:F].

# Core Definition

Let F in K in L be fields. Then [L:F] = [L:K][K:F]. Therefore both [L:K] and [K:F] divide [L:F] (Artin, Theorem 15.3.5). The proof constructs a basis for L/F from bases for L/K and K/F: if (beta_1, ..., beta_n) is a K-basis for L and (alpha_1, ..., alpha_m) is an F-basis for K, then the mn products {alpha_i * beta_j} form an F-basis for L.

# Key Properties

1. Degrees multiply in towers: [L:F] = [L:K][K:F]
2. An element of degree d over F has degree dividing d over any intermediate field K (15.3.6(b))
3. If [K:F] and [K':F] are coprime, then [KK':F] = [K:F][K':F] (15.3.8)
4. A field extension generated by finitely many algebraic elements is finite (15.3.6(c))

# Context & Application

The tower law is the fundamental computational tool in field theory. It is used to determine degrees of composite extensions, to prove elements are algebraic, and is essential for the impossibility proofs of geometric constructions.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 461): [Q(cube_root(2), omega):Q] = 6, since [Q(cube_root(2)):Q] = 3 and omega has degree 2 over Q(cube_root(2)).

**Example 2** (p. 461): If alpha has degree 3 and beta has degree 4 over Q, and gcd(3,4) = 1, then [Q(alpha,beta):Q] = 12.
