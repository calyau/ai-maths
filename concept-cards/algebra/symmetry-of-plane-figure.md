# Quick Definition

A symmetry of a plane figure F is an isometry that carries F to itself. The four basic types are bilateral (reflection), rotational, translational, and glide symmetry.

# Core Definition

A rigid motion of the plane is called an isometry, and if an isometry carries a subset F of the plane to itself, it is called a symmetry of F. The set of all symmetries of F forms a subgroup of the group of all isometries of the plane. This is the group of symmetries of F (Artin, p. 163).

# Prerequisites

- **Isometry** — Symmetries are special isometries

# Key Properties

1. The set of symmetries forms a group under composition
2. Four basic types: bilateral, rotational, translational, and glide
3. Figures may combine multiple types of symmetry
4. Bounded figures have finite symmetry groups (cyclic or dihedral)
5. Unbounded figures may have infinite symmetry groups

# Construction / Recognition

## To Classify:
1. Check for rotational symmetry (what angles?)
2. Check for reflection symmetry (what lines?)
3. Check for translational symmetry (what vectors?)
4. Check for glide symmetry (what lines and vectors?)

# Context & Application

The classification of symmetries of plane figures is one of Artin's signature applications of group theory. It motivates the abstract definition of a group operation (Section 6.7) and provides a rich source of examples. The study leads to the classification of finite groups of orthogonal operators (Section 6.4) and crystallographic groups (Section 6.6).

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 162): A five-pointed star has both bilateral and rotational symmetry; its symmetry group is D_5.

**Example 2** (p. 163): A frieze pattern (6.1.3) extending infinitely in both directions has an infinite cyclic group of translational symmetries generated by a unit translation.

**Example 3** (p. 163): A pattern with glide symmetry (6.1.4) -- the pattern repeats after a combined reflection and translation.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking symmetry means only bilateral (mirror) symmetry
  **Clarification**: Rotational, translational, and glide symmetries are equally valid types
