# Quick Definition

A subring of a ring R is a subset that is closed under addition, subtraction, and multiplication, and that contains the element 1.

# Core Definition

A subring of a ring is a subset that is closed under the operations of addition, subtraction, and multiplication and that contains the element 1 (Artin, p. 339, following Definition 11.1.3).

# Prerequisites

- **Ring** -- Must understand rings to define subsets of them

# Key Properties

1. Closed under addition
2. Closed under subtraction (equivalently, closed under additive inverses)
3. Closed under multiplication
4. Contains the multiplicative identity 1

# Construction / Recognition

## To Recognize:
1. Check the subset contains 1
2. Verify closure under addition and subtraction
3. Verify closure under multiplication

# Context & Application

Subrings arise naturally when restricting attention to subsets of a ring. For example, Z is a subring of Q, which is a subring of R, which is a subring of C.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 339): The Gauss integers Z[i] form a subring of C.

**Example 2** (p. 339): For any complex number alpha, the subring Z[alpha] generated by alpha is the smallest subring of C containing alpha.

# Relationships

## Builds Upon
- **Ring** -- A subring is a special subset of a ring

## Enables
- **Polynomial ring** -- R[x] contains R as a subring of constant polynomials

## Contrasts With
- **Ideal** -- An ideal satisfies subring conditions except it generally does not contain 1

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking an ideal is a subring
  **Clarification**: An ideal I satisfies all subring requirements except containing 1 (unless I = R)
