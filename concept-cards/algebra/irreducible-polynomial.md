# Quick Definition

A polynomial with coefficients in a field is irreducible if it is not constant and cannot be written as the product of two nonconstant polynomials.

# Core Definition

A polynomial with coefficients in a field is called irreducible if it is not constant and if it is not the product of two polynomials, neither of which is a constant (Artin, p. 363). The maximal ideals of F[x] are the principal ideals generated by monic irreducible polynomials (Proposition 11.8.4).

# Prerequisites

- **Polynomial ring** -- Irreducibility is defined for elements of F[x]

# Key Properties

1. In F[x], irreducible <=> prime (since F[x] is a PID)
2. An irreducible polynomial generates a maximal ideal of F[x]
3. F[x]/(p) is a field when p is irreducible
4. Over C, the irreducible polynomials are exactly the linear polynomials
5. Over R, the irreducibles are linear or quadratic with negative discriminant

# Context & Application

Testing irreducibility is a fundamental problem. Key tools: reduction modulo p, the Eisenstein criterion, and the rational root test.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 363): x^2 + 1 is irreducible over R but reducible over C.

**Example 2** (p. 365): Over C, x - a for each a in C are the only monic irreducible polynomials.
