# Quick Definition

The symmetry group of a figure F is the group of all isometries that carry F to itself, with composition as the group operation.

# Core Definition

The set of all symmetries of a subset F of the plane forms a subgroup of the group of all isometries of the plane: if m and m' carry F to F, then so does the composed map mm', and so on. This is the group of symmetries of F (Artin, p. 163).

# Prerequisites

- **Isometry** — Symmetries are isometries
- **Symmetry of a plane figure** — Elements of the symmetry group

# Key Properties

1. Forms a group under composition
2. Subgroup of the isometry group M
3. For bounded figures, the symmetry group is finite (Theorem 6.4.1)
4. For bounded figures, it is either C_n or D_n (Corollary 6.4.10)
5. For unbounded periodic figures, it may be infinite (frieze or wallpaper groups)

# Construction / Recognition

## To Determine:
1. Identify all isometries that map the figure to itself
2. Check closure under composition and inverses
3. For bounded figures: determine rotational order n and presence of reflections

# Context & Application

Symmetry groups are the central object of study in Chapter 6. They connect geometry to algebra by encoding geometric symmetry as algebraic structure. The classification of symmetry groups for bounded figures (C_n or D_n) and for periodic patterns (17 wallpaper groups) are major results.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 163): A frieze pattern has symmetry group generated by a single translation, giving an infinite cyclic group.

**Example 2** (p. 174): A regular n-gon has symmetry group D_n, the dihedral group of order 2n.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Forgetting to include the identity as a symmetry
  **Correction**: The identity isometry always preserves any figure

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking the symmetry group depends on the coordinate system
  **Clarification**: The abstract group structure is independent of coordinates; only the matrix representation changes
