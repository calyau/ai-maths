# Quick Definition

An algebraic variety is a subset of C^n that is the set of common zeros of finitely many polynomials in n variables.

# Core Definition

A subset V of complex n-space C^n that is the set of common zeros of a finite number of polynomials in n variables is called an algebraic variety, or just a variety (Artin, p. 367). The maximal ideals of the quotient ring C[x_1, ..., x_n]/I correspond bijectively to points of V, where I is generated by the defining polynomials (Theorem 11.9.1).

# Prerequisites

- **Polynomial ring in several variables** -- Varieties are zero loci of polynomials
- **Hilbert Nullstellensatz** -- Connects algebra to geometry

# Key Properties

1. Defined as the common zeros of a finite set of polynomials
2. The set of zeros depends only on the ideal generated by the polynomials
3. Maximal ideals of C[x]/I correspond to points of V
4. Two polynomials in two variables have finitely many common zeros unless they share a factor

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 367): A complex line ax + by + c = 0 in C^2 is a variety.

**Example 2** (p. 367): The point (a,b) in C^2 is the variety {x - a = 0, y - b = 0}.

**Example 3** (p. 367): SL_2(C) = {x_{11}x_{22} - x_{12}x_{21} - 1 = 0} in C^{2x2} is a variety.
