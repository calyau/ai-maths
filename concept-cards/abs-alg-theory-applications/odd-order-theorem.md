# Quick Definition

The Odd Order Theorem (Feit-Thompson, 1963) states that every finite simple group of nonprime order must have even order. Equivalently, every group of odd order is solvable.

# Core Definition

**Theorem 15.20 (Odd Order Theorem)**: "Every finite simple group of nonprime order must be of even order" (Judson, p. 201). This was conjectured by Burnside and proved by Feit and Thompson in 1963 in a 255-page paper.

# Prerequisites

- **Simple group** — The theorem is about simple groups
- **Solvable group** — Equivalently, odd-order groups are solvable

# Key Properties

1. Every group of odd order is solvable
2. The only simple groups of odd order are the cyclic groups $\mathbb{Z}_p$
3. The proof was over 250 pages and laid groundwork for the classification of finite simple groups

# Context & Application

The Odd Order Theorem was a landmark in 20th-century mathematics. It implies that the search for nonabelian simple groups can be restricted to groups of even order, greatly simplifying the classification program.
