# Quick Definition

A generator of a cyclic group $G$ is an element $a$ such that $G = \langle a \rangle$; that is, every element of $G$ can be written as a power of $a$.

# Core Definition

"If $G$ contains some element $a$ such that $G = \langle a \rangle$, then $G$ is a cyclic group. In this case $a$ is a generator of $G$" (Judson, p. 60).

**Corollary 4.14**: "The generators of $\mathbb{Z}_n$ are the integers $r$ such that $1 \leq r < n$ and $\gcd(r, n) = 1$" (Judson, p. 62).

# Prerequisites

- **Cyclic group** — Generators are defined for cyclic groups
- **Cyclic subgroup** — A generator makes $\langle a \rangle = G$

# Key Properties

1. A cyclic group can have more than one generator
2. In $\mathbb{Z}_n$: $r$ is a generator iff $\gcd(r, n) = 1$ (Corollary 4.14)
3. $\mathbb{Z}$ has exactly two generators: 1 and $-1$
4. If $b = a^k$ in a cyclic group of order $n$, then $b$ generates $G$ iff $\gcd(k, n) = 1$

# Construction / Recognition

## To Find Generators of $\mathbb{Z}_n$:
1. List integers $r$ with $1 \leq r < n$
2. Keep those with $\gcd(r, n) = 1$
3. These are exactly the generators

## To Check if $a$ Generates $G$:
1. Compute $a, a^2, a^3, \ldots$
2. If all elements of $G$ appear, $a$ is a generator

# Context & Application

Generators determine the structure of cyclic groups. Finding generators is important in cryptography (e.g., finding generators of multiplicative groups for Diffie-Hellman key exchange) and in understanding cyclic group structure.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 60): Both 1 and 5 generate $\mathbb{Z}_6$. But 2 is not a generator: $\langle 2 \rangle = \{0, 2, 4\}$.

**Example 2** (p. 60): $\mathbb{Z}$ is generated by 1 and $-1$.

**Example 3** (p. 62): The generators of $\mathbb{Z}_{16}$ are 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13, 15 (all odd numbers less than 16).

**Example 4** (p. 60): 2 generates $U(9)$: $2^1 = 2$, $2^2 = 4$, $2^3 = 8$, $2^4 = 7$, $2^5 = 5$, $2^6 = 1$.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming every element of a cyclic group is a generator
  **Correction**: Only elements with $\gcd(k, n) = 1$ (where $a^k$ is the element) generate $\mathbb{Z}_n$

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking a cyclic group has a unique generator
  **Clarification**: Cyclic groups typically have multiple generators; $\mathbb{Z}_n$ has $\phi(n)$ generators
