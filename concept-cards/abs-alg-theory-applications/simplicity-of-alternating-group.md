# Quick Definition

The alternating group $A_n$ is simple (has no nontrivial proper normal subgroups) for $n \geq 5$. This is a foundational result in group theory with deep consequences for the unsolvability of polynomial equations of degree 5 and higher.

# Core Definition

**Theorem 10.11**: "The alternating group, $A_n$, is simple for $n \geq 5$" (Judson, p. 142). The proof proceeds through three lemmas: (1) $A_n$ is generated by 3-cycles (Lemma 10.8), (2) any normal subgroup of $A_n$ containing a 3-cycle must be all of $A_n$ (Lemma 10.9), and (3) every nontrivial normal subgroup of $A_n$ contains a 3-cycle for $n \geq 5$ (Lemma 10.10).

# Prerequisites

- **Simple group** — The result asserts simplicity
- **Normal subgroup** — The proof shows no nontrivial normal subgroups exist
- **Alternating group** — The group of even permutations
- **Three-cycle** — Key generators used in the proof

# Key Properties

1. $A_n$ is generated by 3-cycles for $n \geq 3$
2. If a normal subgroup of $A_n$ contains a 3-cycle, it must be all of $A_n$
3. For $n \geq 5$, every nontrivial normal subgroup of $A_n$ contains a 3-cycle
4. $A_3 \cong \mathbb{Z}_3$ is simple (trivially, being of prime order)
5. $A_4$ is NOT simple: $\{(1), (12)(34), (13)(24), (14)(23)\}$ is a normal subgroup
6. $A_5$ was the first nonabelian simple group discovered (by Galois)

# Construction / Recognition

## Proof Strategy (Theorem 10.11):
1. Let $N$ be a normal subgroup of $A_n$ with $n \geq 5$
2. Show $N$ must contain a 3-cycle (Lemma 10.10, using case analysis on cycle structures)
3. Show that any normal subgroup containing a 3-cycle equals $A_n$ (Lemma 10.9)
4. Conclude $N = A_n$, so no proper nontrivial normal subgroups exist

# Context & Application

The simplicity of $A_n$ for $n \geq 5$ is crucial for proving that the general polynomial equation of degree $\geq 5$ cannot be solved by radicals (the Abel-Ruffini theorem, proven via Galois theory). It also means $S_n$ has the composition series $S_n \supset A_n \supset \{(1)\}$ with non-abelian factor $A_n$ for $n \geq 5$, making $S_n$ not solvable.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 140-141): The proof considers five cases for elements $\sigma$ in a normal subgroup $N$: $\sigma$ is a 3-cycle; $\sigma$ contains a cycle of length $> 3$; $\sigma$ is a product of two 3-cycles; $\sigma$ is a 3-cycle times transpositions; $\sigma$ is a product of transpositions. In each case, conjugation produces a 3-cycle in $N$.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming $A_4$ is simple because $A_n$ is simple for $n \geq 5$
  **Correction**: $A_4$ has a proper normal subgroup (the Klein four-group). The bound $n \geq 5$ is sharp.

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking the proof requires checking only specific permutations
  **Clarification**: The proof must handle all possible cycle structures of elements in a normal subgroup
