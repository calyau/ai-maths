# Quick Definition

A subgroup $H$ of a group $G$ is a subset of $G$ that is itself a group under the same operation as $G$.

# Core Definition

"We define a subgroup $H$ of a group $G$ to be a subset $H$ of $G$ such that when the group operation of $G$ is restricted to $H$, $H$ is a group in its own right" (Judson, p. 51). Every group $G$ has at least two subgroups: the trivial subgroup $\{e\}$ and $G$ itself.

# Prerequisites

- **Group** — A subgroup is a subset of a group that is also a group

# Key Properties

1. $H$ must be a subset of $G$
2. $H$ must use the same binary operation as $G$
3. $H$ must satisfy all group axioms under this operation
4. The identity of $H$ must be the identity of $G$ (Proposition 3.30 proof)
5. Inverses in $H$ must equal inverses in $G$

# Construction / Recognition

## To Verify $H$ is a Subgroup (Proposition 3.30):
1. Check $e \in H$ (identity of $G$ is in $H$)
2. Check closure: if $h_1, h_2 \in H$, then $h_1 h_2 \in H$
3. Check inverses: if $h \in H$, then $h^{-1} \in H$

## One-Step Subgroup Test (Proposition 3.31):
1. Check $H \neq \emptyset$
2. Check: if $g, h \in H$, then $gh^{-1} \in H$

# Context & Application

Subgroups are fundamental to understanding group structure. Every theorem about groups applies to subgroups. Finding all subgroups of a group reveals its internal structure. Different subgroup structures distinguish non-isomorphic groups.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 51): $\mathbb{Q}^*$ is a subgroup of $\mathbb{R}^*$ under multiplication.

**Example 2** (p. 51): $H = \{1, -1, i, -i\}$ is a subgroup of $\mathbb{C}^*$.

**Example 3** (p. 52): $\mathbb{Z}_4$ has one nontrivial proper subgroup $\{0, 2\}$, while $\mathbb{Z}_2 \times \mathbb{Z}_2$ has three nontrivial proper subgroups. This distinguishes the two groups.

**Non-Example** (p. 52): $GL_2(\mathbb{R})$ under multiplication is a subset of $M_2(\mathbb{R})$ and a group, but NOT a subgroup of $(M_2(\mathbb{R}), +)$ because the operations differ.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Checking only closure and forgetting identity and inverses
  **Correction**: All three conditions of Proposition 3.30 must be verified (or use the one-step test)

- **Error**: Claiming $H$ is a subgroup when $H$ uses a different operation than $G$
  **Correction**: The subgroup must use the same operation as the parent group

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking any subset that is a group is a subgroup
  **Clarification**: The subset must be a group under the SAME operation as $G$, not a different one
