# Quick Definition

The $n$th roots of unity are the $n$ complex numbers satisfying $z^n = 1$. They form a cyclic subgroup of $\mathbb{T}$ of order $n$, given by $z = \operatorname{cis}(2k\pi/n)$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$.

# Core Definition

"The complex numbers satisfying the equation $z^n = 1$ are called the $n$th roots of unity" (Judson, p. 65).

**Theorem 4.25**: "If $z^n = 1$, then the $n$th roots of unity are $z = \operatorname{cis}(2k\pi/n)$, where $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$. Furthermore, the $n$th roots of unity form a cyclic subgroup of $\mathbb{T}$ of order $n$" (Judson, p. 65).

# Prerequisites

- **DeMoivre's Theorem** — Used in the proof
- **Circle group** — Roots of unity are a subgroup of $\mathbb{T}$
- **Cyclic group** — The roots of unity form a cyclic group

# Key Properties

1. There are exactly $n$ distinct $n$th roots of unity
2. They are equally spaced on the unit circle
3. They form a cyclic group of order $n$
4. $\omega = \operatorname{cis}(2\pi/n)$ is a primitive $n$th root of unity (a generator)
5. The $n$th roots are $1, \omega, \omega^2, \ldots, \omega^{n-1}$

# Construction / Recognition

## To Find the $n$th Roots of Unity:
1. Set $\omega = \operatorname{cis}(2\pi/n)$
2. The roots are $\omega^k = \operatorname{cis}(2k\pi/n)$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$

# Context & Application

Roots of unity provide concrete examples of finite cyclic groups embedded in $\mathbb{C}^*$. They appear in discrete Fourier transforms, cyclotomic polynomials, and algebraic number theory.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 65): The 4th roots of unity are $\{1, i, -1, -i\}$ forming a cyclic group generated by $i$.

**Example 2** (p. 66): The 8th roots of unity are 8 equally spaced points on the unit circle. The primitive 8th roots are $\omega, \omega^3, \omega^5, \omega^7$ where $\omega = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} + \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}i$.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Forgetting that $k = 0$ gives the root $z = 1$
  **Correction**: 1 is always an $n$th root of unity

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking roots of unity are real numbers
  **Clarification**: For $n \geq 3$, most roots of unity are complex (non-real)
