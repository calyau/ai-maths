# Quick Definition

A nonzero, non-unit element $p$ in an integral domain $D$ is prime if whenever $p | ab$, either $p | a$ or $p | b$.

# Core Definition

"Furthermore, $p$ is prime if whenever $p | ab$ either $p | a$ or $p | b$" (Judson, p. 242).

# Prerequisites

- **Integral domain** — Primality is defined in integral domains

# Key Properties

1. Nonzero and not a unit
2. $p | ab \Rightarrow p | a$ or $p | b$
3. Every prime element is irreducible (in an integral domain)
4. The converse fails in general (Example 18.8)
5. In a PID, irreducible $\Leftrightarrow$ prime (Corollary 18.13)

# Construction / Recognition

## To Verify:
1. Confirm $p$ is nonzero and not a unit
2. Show $p | ab$ implies $p | a$ or $p | b$

# Context & Application

The prime property is crucial for unique factorization. The distinction between prime and irreducible is one of the subtleties of factorization theory outside PIDs.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 243): In $\mathbb{Z}$, prime elements are $\pm 2, \pm 3, \pm 5, \ldots$ (same as irreducibles).

**Example 2** (p. 242): In the subring of $\mathbb{Q}[x,y]$ generated by $x^2, y^2, xy$: $xy$ is irreducible but not prime since $xy | x^2y^2$ but $xy \nmid x^2$ and $xy \nmid y^2$.

# Relationships

## Related
- **Prime ideal** — $p$ is prime iff $\langle p \rangle$ is a prime ideal
- **Irreducible element** — Prime $\Rightarrow$ irreducible; converse holds in PIDs

## Contrasts With
- **Irreducible element** — Irreducible need not be prime in general domains

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming prime and irreducible are synonymous
  **Correction**: They agree in PIDs (including $\mathbb{Z}$ and $F[x]$) but not in all integral domains

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Confusing "prime element" with "prime ideal"
  **Clarification**: A prime element generates a prime ideal, but prime ideals need not be principal
