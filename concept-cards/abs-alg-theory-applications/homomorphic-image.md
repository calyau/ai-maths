# Quick Definition

The homomorphic image of a group homomorphism $\phi: G \to H$ is the range $\phi(G) = \{\phi(g) : g \in G\}$, which is always a subgroup of $H$.

# Core Definition

"The range of $\phi$ in $H$ is called the **homomorphic image** of $\phi$" (Judson, p. 146). By Proposition 11.4, if $H_1$ is a subgroup of $G_1$, then $\phi(H_1)$ is a subgroup of $G_2$. In particular, $\phi(G)$ is a subgroup of $H$.

# Prerequisites

- **Group homomorphism** — The image is defined for a homomorphism

# Key Properties

1. $\phi(G)$ is always a subgroup of $H$
2. If $G$ is abelian, then $\phi(G)$ is abelian
3. If $G$ is cyclic, then $\phi(G)$ is cyclic
4. $|\phi(G)|$ divides $|G|$ (when $G$ is finite)
5. By the First Isomorphism Theorem, $\phi(G) \cong G/\ker\phi$

# Construction / Recognition

## To Compute the Homomorphic Image:
1. Apply $\phi$ to every element of $G$
2. Collect all distinct outputs to form $\phi(G)$
3. For practical computation, apply $\phi$ to generators and take the generated subgroup

# Context & Application

The homomorphic image captures how much of the structure of $G$ is "seen" by the target group $H$. The First Isomorphism Theorem establishes that $\phi(G) \cong G/\ker\phi$, connecting images to factor groups.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 146): The homomorphism $\phi: \mathbb{Z} \to G$ defined by $\phi(n) = g^n$ has image $\langle g \rangle$, the cyclic subgroup generated by $g$.

**Example 2** (p. 146): The determinant map $\phi: GL_2(\mathbb{R}) \to \mathbb{R}^*$ has image all of $\mathbb{R}^*$ (it is surjective).

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking the image of a homomorphism must be all of $H$
  **Clarification**: The image $\phi(G)$ is a subgroup of $H$, but need not equal $H$. A homomorphism whose image is all of $H$ is called surjective.
