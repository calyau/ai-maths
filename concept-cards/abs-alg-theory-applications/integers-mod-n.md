# Quick Definition

The integers modulo $n$, denoted $\mathbb{Z}_n$, is the set of $n$ equivalence classes $\{0, 1, 2, \ldots, n-1\}$ of integers under congruence mod $n$, equipped with addition modulo $n$.

# Core Definition

$\mathbb{Z}_n$ consists of the equivalence classes of the integers modulo $n$. "We can do arithmetic on $\mathbb{Z}_n$. For two integers $a$ and $b$, define addition modulo $n$ to be $(a + b) \pmod{n}$; that is, the remainder when $a + b$ is divided by $n$. Similarly, multiplication modulo $n$ is defined as $(ab) \pmod{n}$" (Judson, p. 42). By Proposition 3.4, $\mathbb{Z}_n = \{0, 1, \ldots, n-1\}$ forms a group under addition mod $n$.

# Prerequisites

- **Congruence modulo n** — $\mathbb{Z}_n$ consists of equivalence classes under this relation
- **Equivalence class** — Elements of $\mathbb{Z}_n$ are equivalence classes

# Key Properties

1. Contains exactly $n$ elements: $\{[0], [1], \ldots, [n-1]\}$
2. Forms a group under addition modulo $n$
3. Addition and multiplication are commutative and associative
4. The additive identity is 0; the additive inverse of $a$ is $n - a$
5. Multiplicative inverses exist for $a$ if and only if $\gcd(a, n) = 1$
6. The product of two nonzero elements can be zero (e.g., $3 \cdot 4 \equiv 0 \pmod{12}$)

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct $\mathbb{Z}_n$:
1. Take the set $\{0, 1, 2, \ldots, n-1\}$
2. Define $a + b$ as the remainder of $a + b$ divided by $n$
3. Define $a \cdot b$ as the remainder of $a \cdot b$ divided by $n$

# Context & Application

$\mathbb{Z}_n$ is one of the most important examples of a group in abstract algebra. It is a cyclic group generated by 1. It is used in cryptography, coding theory, and error detection. $\mathbb{Z}_n$ also serves as the prototypical example of a cyclic group.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 42): In $\mathbb{Z}_5$: $7 + 4 \equiv 1 \pmod{5}$ and $7 \cdot 3 \equiv 1 \pmod{5}$.

**Example 2** (p. 42): In $\mathbb{Z}_{12}$: $3 \cdot 4 \equiv 0 \pmod{12}$, showing the product of nonzero elements can be zero.

**Example 3** (p. 42): In $\mathbb{Z}_8$, elements 2, 4, 6 have no multiplicative inverse because they are not relatively prime to 8.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming all nonzero elements of $\mathbb{Z}_n$ have multiplicative inverses
  **Correction**: Only elements relatively prime to $n$ have multiplicative inverses

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Confusing $\mathbb{Z}_n$ as a group under multiplication vs. addition
  **Clarification**: $\mathbb{Z}_n$ is a group under addition. Under multiplication, only $U(n)$ (elements coprime to $n$) forms a group.
