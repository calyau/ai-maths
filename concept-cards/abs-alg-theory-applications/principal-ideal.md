# Quick Definition

A principal ideal in a commutative ring $R$ with identity is an ideal generated by a single element $a$: $\langle a \rangle = \{ar : r \in R\}$.

# Core Definition

"If $R$ is a commutative ring with identity, then an ideal of the form $\langle a \rangle = \{ar : r \in R\}$ is called a principal ideal" (Judson, p. 210). Every ideal in $\mathbb{Z}$ is principal (Theorem 16.25).

# Prerequisites

- **Ideal** — A principal ideal is a special type of ideal
- **Commutative ring** — Principal ideals are typically studied in commutative rings
- **Ring with unity** — The definition uses the identity element

# Key Properties

1. $\langle a \rangle = \{ar : r \in R\}$
2. $0 \in \langle a \rangle$ and $a \in \langle a \rangle$
3. Every ideal in $\mathbb{Z}$ is principal (Theorem 16.25)
4. $a | b$ iff $\langle b \rangle \subset \langle a \rangle$ (Lemma 18.11)
5. $a$ is a unit iff $\langle a \rangle = R$ (Lemma 18.11)

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct:
1. Choose a generator $a \in R$
2. Form $\langle a \rangle = \{ar : r \in R\}$

# Context & Application

Principal ideals are the simplest type of ideal. Rings where every ideal is principal (principal ideal domains) have particularly nice factorization properties. The study of principal ideals leads to the hierarchy: Euclidean domains $\subset$ PIDs $\subset$ UFDs.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 210): In $\mathbb{Z}$, $\langle n \rangle = n\mathbb{Z}$ for any integer $n$.

**Example 2** (p. 210): In $\mathbb{Z}$, every ideal is principal by Theorem 16.25.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming every ideal in every ring is principal
  **Correction**: In $\mathbb{Z}[x]$, the ideal $\{5f(x) + xg(x)\}$ is not principal (Example 18.17)

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Confusing the ideal $\langle a \rangle$ with the set $\{a\}$
  **Clarification**: $\langle a \rangle$ consists of all multiples of $a$, not just $a$ itself
