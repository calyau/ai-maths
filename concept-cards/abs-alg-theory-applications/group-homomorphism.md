# Quick Definition

A group homomorphism is a map $\phi: G \to H$ between groups that preserves the group operation: $\phi(g_1 \cdot g_2) = \phi(g_1) \circ \phi(g_2)$. It is a generalization of an isomorphism that does not require bijectivity.

# Core Definition

"A **homomorphism** between groups $(G, \cdot)$ and $(H, \circ)$ is a map $\phi: G \to H$ such that $\phi(g_1 \cdot g_2) = \phi(g_1) \circ \phi(g_2)$ for $g_1, g_2 \in G$" (Judson, p. 146). "One of the basic ideas of algebra is the concept of a homomorphism, a natural generalization of an isomorphism. If we relax the requirement that an isomorphism of groups be bijective, we have a homomorphism."

# Prerequisites

- **Group** — Source and target must be groups

# Key Properties

1. $\phi(e_G) = e_H$ (identity maps to identity, Proposition 11.4)
2. $\phi(g^{-1}) = [\phi(g)]^{-1}$ (inverses map to inverses)
3. If $H_1$ is a subgroup of $G$, then $\phi(H_1)$ is a subgroup of $H$
4. If $H_2$ is a subgroup of $H$, then $\phi^{-1}(H_2)$ is a subgroup of $G$
5. If $H_2$ is normal in $H$, then $\phi^{-1}(H_2)$ is normal in $G$
6. A homomorphism is injective if and only if $\ker\phi = \{e\}$

# Construction / Recognition

## To Verify a Map is a Homomorphism:
1. Confirm that the domain and codomain are groups
2. Show $\phi(g_1 g_2) = \phi(g_1)\phi(g_2)$ for all $g_1, g_2$ in the domain
3. The preservation of identity and inverses follows automatically

## To Construct a Homomorphism:
1. Define $\phi$ on generators and extend by the homomorphism property
2. Verify well-definedness if the domain has relations

# Context & Application

Homomorphisms are fundamental to algebra because they reveal structural relationships between groups. Every homomorphism factors through a factor group via the First Isomorphism Theorem. The kernel-image correspondence connects normal subgroups and factor groups to homomorphisms.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 146): For any group $G$ and $g \in G$, the map $\phi: \mathbb{Z} \to G$ defined by $\phi(n) = g^n$ is a homomorphism. It maps $\mathbb{Z}$ onto the cyclic subgroup generated by $g$.

**Example 2** (p. 146): The determinant map $\phi: GL_2(\mathbb{R}) \to \mathbb{R}^*$ defined by $A \mapsto \det(A)$ is a homomorphism since $\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$.

**Example 3** (p. 147): The map $\phi: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{T}$ defined by $\phi(\theta) = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ is a homomorphism from the additive reals to the circle group.

# Relationships

## Builds Upon
- **Group** — Homomorphisms map between groups

## Enables
- **Kernel of homomorphism** — The preimage of the identity
- **First isomorphism theorem** — $G/\ker\phi \cong \phi(G)$
- **Factor group** — Every normal subgroup is a kernel, giving rise to a factor group

## Related
- **Homomorphic image** — The range $\phi(G)$
- **Canonical homomorphism** — The natural map $G \to G/N$

## Contrasts With
- **Isomorphism** — An isomorphism is a bijective homomorphism

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Forgetting to check that the map respects the group operations of both domain and codomain
  **Correction**: The operation in $\phi(g_1 \cdot g_2) = \phi(g_1) \circ \phi(g_2)$ may differ between domain and codomain (e.g., addition vs. multiplication)

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Believing every homomorphism is an isomorphism
  **Clarification**: A homomorphism need not be injective or surjective. It is an isomorphism only when it is both.

- **Confusion**: Thinking $\phi(e) = e$ must be checked separately
  **Clarification**: This follows automatically from the homomorphism property (Proposition 11.4)
