# Quick Definition

Every subgroup of a cyclic group is itself cyclic. The subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}$ are exactly $n\mathbb{Z}$ for $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$

# Core Definition

**Theorem 4.10**: "Every subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic" (Judson, p. 61).

**Corollary 4.11**: "The subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}$ are exactly $n\mathbb{Z}$ for $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$" (Judson, p. 61).

**Theorem 4.13**: In a cyclic group of order $n$ generated by $a$, if $b = a^k$ then $|b| = n/d$ where $d = \gcd(k, n)$ (Judson, p. 62).

# Prerequisites

- **Cyclic group** — The theorem applies to cyclic groups
- **Subgroup** — Characterizes subgroups of cyclic groups

# Key Properties

1. Every subgroup of a cyclic group is cyclic
2. The subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}$ are $n\mathbb{Z} = \{nk : k \in \mathbb{Z}\}$ for $n \geq 0$
3. In $\mathbb{Z}_n$, the order of $a^k$ is $n/\gcd(k, n)$
4. The generators of $\mathbb{Z}_n$ are exactly the $r$ with $\gcd(r, n) = 1$
5. The proof uses the Division Algorithm and Well-Ordering Principle

# Construction / Recognition

## To Find All Subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}_n$:
1. For each divisor $d$ of $n$, there is exactly one subgroup of order $d$
2. This subgroup is $\langle n/d \rangle$
3. There are no other subgroups

# Context & Application

This theorem greatly simplifies the study of cyclic groups: their subgroup structure is completely determined by the divisors of the group order. This is a key structural result that distinguishes cyclic groups from more complex groups.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 61): The subgroups of $\mathbb{Z}$ are $0\mathbb{Z} = \{0\}$, $1\mathbb{Z} = \mathbb{Z}$, $2\mathbb{Z}$, $3\mathbb{Z}$, etc.

**Example 2** (p. 60): The subgroups of $S_3$ are all cyclic: $\{e\}$, $\langle \rho_1 \rangle = \{e, \rho_1, \rho_2\}$, $\langle \mu_1 \rangle = \{e, \mu_1\}$, $\langle \mu_2 \rangle = \{e, \mu_2\}$, $\langle \mu_3 \rangle = \{e, \mu_3\}$, but $S_3$ itself is not cyclic.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming subgroups of non-cyclic groups are always cyclic
  **Correction**: The theorem applies ONLY to cyclic groups; non-cyclic groups can have non-cyclic subgroups

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking a group whose subgroups are all cyclic must itself be cyclic
  **Clarification**: $S_3$ has all cyclic proper subgroups but is not cyclic
