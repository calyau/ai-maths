# Quick Definition
A graph is 3-connected if it has at least 4 vertices and remains connected after the removal of any two vertices (kappa(G) >= 3).

# Core Definition
A graph G is **3-connected** if |G| >= 4 and G - {u,v} is connected for every pair of vertices u, v. Equivalently, kappa(G) >= 3.

# Prerequisites
- **2-connected graph** — 3-connectivity is a strengthening
- **Connectivity** — kappa(G) >= 3

# Key Properties
1. |G| >= 4 required (K4 is the smallest 3-connected graph)
2. Cannot be disconnected by removing fewer than 3 vertices
3. Every 3-connected graph can be constructed from K4 by successive vertex splitting (Theorem 3.2.2)
4. Has a contractible edge: an edge e such that G/e is still 3-connected (Lemma 3.2.1, for |G| > 4)
5. The cycle space is generated by non-separating induced cycles (Theorem 3.2.3)

# Construction / Recognition
## Constructive Characterization (Theorem 3.2.2)
1. Start with K4
2. Pick a vertex v, split it into v' and v'' joined by an edge
3. Distribute neighbors of v between v' and v''
4. Ensure both v' and v'' have degree >= 3
5. Ensure every former neighbor of v is adjacent to at least one of v', v''
6. Repeat to build any 3-connected graph

# Context & Application
3-connected graphs are important because they have more rigid structure than 2-connected graphs. Tutte's wheel theorem provides a complete inductive characterization. The 3-connected graphs include all wheels and are the "atomic" pieces in the canonical tree-decomposition of 2-connected graphs.

# Examples
**Example**: K4 is the smallest 3-connected graph. Wheels C^n * K^1 are 3-connected. The Petersen graph is 3-connected.

# Common Errors
- **Error**: Thinking K3 (triangle) is 3-connected
  **Correction**: K3 has only 3 vertices; 3-connected requires |G| >= 4

# Common Confusions
- **Confusion**: Confusing 3-connected with 3-edge-connected
  **Clarification**: 3-connected means removing any 2 vertices leaves the graph connected; 3-edge-connected means removing any 2 edges leaves it connected
