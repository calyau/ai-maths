# Quick Definition
Extremal graph theory asks how many edges force a subgraph H; Ramsey theory asks how large G must be so that EVERY 2-colouring yields a monochromatic H. Despite superficial similarity, they lead to very different mathematics.

# Core Definition
The chapter introduction explains: "In this chapter we set out from a type of problem which, on the face of it, appears to be similar to the theme of the last chapter: what kind of substructures are necessarily present in every large enough graph?" However, "despite its superficial similarity to extremal problems, the above type of question leads to a kind of mathematics with a distinctive flavour of its own." (Diestel, p. 251)

The key difference: Ramsey theory asks about COLOURED substructures, not just substructure existence.

# Prerequisites
- **Ramsey's theorem (finite)** — The starting point of Ramsey theory
- **Turan's theorem** — The starting point of extremal theory

# Key Properties
1. Extremal: edge counts -> subgraph containment (one structure)
2. Ramsey: vertex counts -> monochromatic subgraph (two-coloured structure)
3. Ramsey results have "more in common with similar results in algebra or geometry"
4. The regularity lemma bridges both areas (used in Theorem 9.2.2)
