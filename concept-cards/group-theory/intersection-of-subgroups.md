# Quick Definition

An intersection of any collection of subgroups of a group G is again a subgroup of G. This fundamental property enables the definition of the subgroup generated by a set.

# Core Definition

An intersection of subgroups of G is a subgroup of G (Proposition 1.13, p. 12). The proof is immediate: the intersection is nonempty because it contains e, and conditions (S1) and (S2) obviously hold.

This is an instance of a general pattern: an intersection of subobjects of an algebraic object is a subobject. For example, intersections of subrings are subrings, intersections of submodules are submodules (Remark 1.14, p. 12).

# Key Properties

1. The intersection of any (possibly infinite) family of subgroups is a subgroup
2. The intersection contains e (nonemptiness)
3. Closure under multiplication and inverses is inherited from each subgroup

# Construction / Recognition

## To Verify:
1. Each subgroup in the family contains e, so the intersection is nonempty
2. If a, b are in the intersection, they are in every subgroup, so ab is in every subgroup
3. If a is in the intersection, a^{-1} is in every subgroup

# Context & Application

This result is the foundation for defining the subgroup generated by a set: <X> is the intersection of all subgroups containing X (Proposition 1.15). Without this closure property of intersections, the notion of "smallest subgroup containing X" would not be well-defined.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 12): The subgroup generated by X is defined as the intersection of all subgroups of G containing X.

- **Confusion**: Thinking the union of subgroups is a subgroup
