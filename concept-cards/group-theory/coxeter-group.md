# Quick Definition

A Coxeter group is a group that arises as the group component of a Coxeter system -- i.e., a group generated by involutions $S$ with only relations of the form $(st)^{m(s,t)} = 1$.

# Core Definition

"The *Coxeter groups* are those that arise as part of a Coxeter system." (Milne, p. 38)

A Coxeter group is $G = \langle S \mid (st)^{m(s,t)}, \text{ for } m(s,t) \neq \infty \rangle$.

# Prerequisites

- **Coxeter system** — a Coxeter group is the group part of a Coxeter system

# Key Properties

1. Each generator has order exactly 2 (Theorem 2.16b)
2. For $s \neq t$, the product $st$ has order exactly $m(s,t)$ (Theorem 2.16c)
3. The generators remain distinct in $G$ (Theorem 2.16a)
4. Finite Coxeter groups = finite reflection groups (Humphreys 1990, 6.4)
5. The representation $G \to \mathrm{GL}(V)$ in the proof of Theorem 2.16 is injective (Remark 2.19)

# Context & Application

Coxeter groups encompass symmetric groups, dihedral groups, and Weyl groups. They are central to the classification of semisimple Lie algebras and algebraic groups. Every finite reflection group is a Coxeter group.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 39): $S_n$ is a Coxeter group -- the transpositions $(i, i+1)$ generate $S_n$, and $S_n$ acts on $\mathbb{R}^n$ by permuting coordinates, making it a reflection group.

**Example 2** (p. 39): $D_n$ is a Coxeter group of rank 2 with $m(s,t) = n$.
