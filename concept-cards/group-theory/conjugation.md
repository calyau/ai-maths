# Quick Definition

Conjugation by $g$ is the map $a \mapsto gag^{-1}$. A subgroup is normal precisely when it is invariant under all conjugations. A subset $X$ is normal (closed under conjugation) if $gXg^{-1} \subset X$ for all $g$.

# Core Definition

"The map 'conjugation by $g$', $a \mapsto gag^{-1}$, is a homomorphism $G \to G$." (Milne, Lemma 1.38, p. 19)

A subset $X$ of $G$ is *normal* (or *closed under conjugation*) if $gXg^{-1} \subset X$ for all $g \in G$. (Milne, p. 19)

# Prerequisites

- **Group** — Conjugation is defined in any group

# Key Properties

1. Conjugation by $g$ is an automorphism of $G$ (it is bijective with inverse "conjugation by $g^{-1}$")
2. $N \trianglelefteq G \iff gNg^{-1} = N$ for all $g$
3. If $X$ is closed under conjugation, then $\langle X \rangle$ is a normal subgroup (Lemma 1.38)
4. The smallest normal set containing $X$ is $\bigcup_{g \in G} gXg^{-1}$ (Lemma 1.39)
5. The normal subgroup generated by $X$ is $\langle \bigcup_{g \in G} gXg^{-1} \rangle$ (Proposition 1.40)

# Construction / Recognition

## The Conjugation Map:
1. Fix $g \in G$
2. Define $\iota_g: G \to G$ by $\iota_g(a) = gag^{-1}$
3. This is a group automorphism (inner automorphism)

# Context & Application

Conjugation is the fundamental operation connecting elements, subgroups, and normality. The orbits of elements under conjugation are conjugacy classes, which play a central role in representation theory and the structure of finite groups.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 18): In $D_n$, $srs^{-1} = r^{-1}$ and $r^{-1}sr = r^{n-2}s$.

**Example 2** (p. 18): In $\mathrm{GL}_2(\mathbb{Q})$, conjugation by $g = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ sends $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & n \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ to $\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 5n \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, showing that $gHg^{-1} \subsetneq H$ is possible for infinite groups (Example 1.33).

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Assuming $gHg^{-1} \subset H$ implies $gHg^{-1} = H$
  **Correction**: This implication holds for finite $H$ but can fail for infinite $H$ (Example 1.33)
