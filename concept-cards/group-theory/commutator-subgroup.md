# Quick Definition

The commutator subgroup G' = G^(1) is the subgroup generated by all commutators [x, y] in G. It is the smallest normal subgroup of G such that G/G' is commutative, and it is characteristic in G.

# Core Definition

The group G' = G^(1) generated by the commutators in G is called the *commutator* or *first derived subgroup* of G.

**Proposition 6.9.** The commutator subgroup G' is a characteristic subgroup of G; it is the smallest normal subgroup of G such that G/G' is commutative.

(Milne, p. 91, Proposition 6.9)

# Prerequisites

- **commutator** -- G' is generated by commutators
- **characteristic-subgroup** -- G' is characteristic (stable under all automorphisms)

# Key Properties

1. G' is characteristic in G (any automorphism maps commutators to commutators)
2. G/G' is the largest abelian quotient of G (the "abelianization")
3. N normal in G with G/N abelian implies N contains G'
4. G is commutative iff G' = {1}
5. For n >= 5, (S_n)' = A_n
6. Not every element of G' is a commutator (smallest counterexample at order 96, Aside 6.11)

# Construction / Recognition

## To Compute G':
1. List all commutators [x, y] for x, y in G
2. G' is the subgroup generated by these commutators
3. For matrix groups, commutators often have a clean description (e.g., SL_n for GL_n)

## Universal Property:
Any homomorphism from G to an abelian group factors uniquely through G/G'.

# Context & Application

The commutator subgroup is the foundation of the derived series and hence of the theory of solvable groups. G/G' is the abelianization of G, which captures the "abelian part" of G's structure.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 91): For S_n with n >= 5, (S_n)' = A_n since A_n is the smallest normal subgroup giving a commutative quotient.

**Example 2**: For GL_n(F), the commutator subgroup is SL_n(F) (for n >= 2, F a field with more than 2 elements).

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking every element of G' is a commutator
  **Clarification**: G' is *generated* by commutators, but not every element need be a single commutator. The smallest counterexample has order 96 (Aside 6.11). However, for simple groups this does hold (Ore's conjecture, proved 2008).
