# Quick Definition

The complete list of finite simple groups consists of cyclic groups of prime order, alternating groups $A_n$ ($n \geq 5$), groups of Lie type, and 26 sporadic groups.

# Core Definition

"There is a complete list of finite simple groups. They are (a) the cyclic groups of prime order, (b) the alternating groups $A_n$ for $n \geq 5$, (c) certain infinite families of matrix groups (said to be of Lie type), and (d) the 26 'sporadic groups'" (Milne, p. 51).

# Prerequisites

- **Simple group** — These are the groups being classified
- **Holder program** — This classification is Part A of the program

# Key Properties

1. Classification was announced in 1982
2. Proof confirmed complete with Aschbacher-Smith 2004
3. The proof spans thousands of pages across journal articles
4. Key strategy: study centralizers of elements of order 2 (involution centralizers)
5. Feit-Thompson theorem (1963): every finite simple group of even order contains an involution
6. Every finite simple group can be generated by two elements

# Context & Application

The classification is one of the greatest achievements of 20th-century mathematics. It provides the complete list of "atoms" from which all finite groups are built via extensions. The proof relies on the strategy of Brauer and others, analyzing involution centralizers.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 51): $\operatorname{PSL}_n(\mathbb{F}_q) = \operatorname{SL}_n(\mathbb{F}_q)/\{\text{centre}\}$ is simple for $n \geq 3$ and for $n = 2$, $q > 3$.

**Example 2** (p. 52): The smallest noncommutative simple group is $A_5$; the second smallest is $\operatorname{PSL}_3(\mathbb{F}_2)$ of order 168.
