# Quick Definition

The alternating group $A_n$ is the kernel of the sign homomorphism $\operatorname{sign}: S_n \to \{\pm 1\}$, consisting of all even permutations.

# Core Definition

"When $n \geq 2$, [the sign homomorphism] is surjective, and so its kernel is a normal subgroup of $S_n$ of order $\frac{n!}{2}$, called the alternating group $A_n$" (Milne, p. 65).

# Prerequisites

- **Sign of a permutation** — $A_n$ is the kernel of the sign map
- **Permutation group** — $A_n$ is a subgroup of $S_n$

# Key Properties

1. $|A_n| = n!/2$ for $n \geq 2$
2. $A_n \trianglelefteq S_n$ with index 2
3. $A_n$ is generated by 3-cycles (Corollary 4.28)
4. $A_n$ is simple for $n \geq 5$ (Theorem 4.33, Galois)
5. $A_n$ is not solvable for $n \geq 5$ (Aside 4.39)
6. $S_n = A_n \rtimes C_2$ (Example 3.9b)

# Construction / Recognition

## To verify $\sigma \in A_n$:
1. Decompose $\sigma$ into disjoint cycles
2. Check that the total number of even-length cycles is even
3. Alternatively: decompose into transpositions and check the count is even

# Context & Application

$A_n$ is one of the infinite families of finite simple groups (for $n \geq 5$). Its simplicity is the key to proving that the general polynomial of degree $\geq 5$ is not solvable by radicals (Galois theory).

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 68): $A_4$ has order 12. It contains 1, eight 3-cycles, and three elements of type $(ab)(cd)$. The elements of type $(ab)(cd)$ together with 1 form the Klein four-group $V \trianglelefteq A_4$.

**Example 2** (p. 66): Every element of $A_n$ can be written as a product of 3-cycles: $(ij)(kl) = (ijk)(jkl)$ when $i,j,k,l$ are distinct.

- **Error**: Assuming $A_n$ is simple for all $n$
