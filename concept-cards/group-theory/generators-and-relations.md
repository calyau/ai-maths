# Quick Definition

A group can be described by specifying a set $X$ of generators and a set $R$ of relations (words in $X'$). The group is the quotient of the free group $F_X$ by the normal subgroup generated by the elements of $R$.

# Core Definition

"Consider a set $X$ and a set $R$ of words made up of symbols in $X'$. Each element of $R$ represents an element of the free group $F_X$, and the quotient $G$ of $F_X$ by the normal subgroup generated by these elements (1.40) is said to have $X$ as **generators** and $R$ as **relations** (or as a **set of defining relations**)." (Milne, p. 35)

# Prerequisites

- **Free group** — the group is a quotient of a free group
- **Normal subgroup generated by a set** — relations generate a normal subgroup
- **Quotient group** — the presented group is a quotient

# Key Properties

1. The group $G = F_X / N$, where $N = \langle\!\langle R \rangle\!\rangle$ (normal subgroup generated by $R$ in $F_X$)
2. Universal property (Proposition 2.8): for any map $\alpha: X \to H$ sending each relation to 1, there exists a unique homomorphism $G \to H$
3. Relations are words that become trivial in $G$
4. The group may satisfy additional relations beyond those in $R$ (consequences of $R$)

# Construction / Recognition

## To Construct a Group from Generators and Relations:
1. Form the free group $F_X$ on the generators
2. Let $N$ be the normal subgroup of $F_X$ generated by the relation words
3. The presented group is $G = F_X / N$

# Context & Application

This is the standard way to define groups abstractly. Many important groups (dihedral, Coxeter, fundamental groups of surfaces) are naturally described by generators and relations.

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 35): $D_n$ has generators $r, s$ and relations $r^n, s^2, srsr$ (i.e., $r^n = 1$, $s^2 = 1$, $srsr = 1$).

**Example 2** (p. 35): $\langle s, t \mid s^3t, t^3, s^4 \rangle = \{1\}$ (the trivial group).

**Example 3** (p. 35): The free abelian group on $a_1, \ldots, a_n$ has relations $[a_i, a_j]$ for $i \neq j$.

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Thinking the relations $R$ are the *only* identities in $G$
  **Correction**: $R$ generates additional consequences. E.g., if $a^3 = 1$ and $a^2 = 1$ are in $R$, then $a = 1$ follows
