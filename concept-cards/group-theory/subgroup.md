# Quick Definition

A subgroup of a group G is a nonempty subset S that is closed under multiplication and taking inverses, so that S itself forms a group under the same operation. The notation H <= G means H is a subgroup of G.

# Core Definition

A nonempty subset S of a group G satisfying:

- **(S1)**: a, b in S implies ab in S (closure under multiplication), and
- **(S2)**: a in S implies a^{-1} in S (closure under inverses)

is called a **subgroup** of G. Condition (S1) ensures the binary operation restricts to S x S -> S, associativity is inherited, and (S2) ensures inverses lie in S. The element e belongs to S because S is nonempty and contains some a, its inverse a^{-1}, and the product e = a * a^{-1} (Proposition 1.11, p. 11).

When S is **finite**, condition (S1) alone implies (S2): if a in S, then {a, a^2, ...} is contained in S, so a has finite order n with a^n = e, and a^{-1} = a^{n-1} in S. The example (N, +) subset of (Z, +) shows (S1) does not imply (S2) for infinite S (p. 11).

# Prerequisites

- **Group** — a subgroup is a subset of a group
- **Neutral element** — the subgroup must contain e
- **Inverse element** — the subgroup must be closed under inverses

# Key Properties

1. A subgroup is itself a group under the inherited operation
2. Every subgroup contains the neutral element e
3. For finite subsets, closure (S1) automatically implies inverses (S2)
4. For infinite subsets, both (S1) and (S2) must be checked
5. {e} and G are always subgroups of G (trivial subgroups)
6. An intersection of subgroups is a subgroup (Proposition 1.13)

# Construction / Recognition

## To Verify S is a Subgroup:
1. Check S is nonempty
2. Check (S1): for all a, b in S, ab is in S
3. Check (S2): for all a in S, a^{-1} is in S
4. (If S is finite, step 3 is automatic from step 2)

# Context & Application

Subgroups are the primary structural tool in group theory. They enable the study of a group by examining its internal structure. Key results like Lagrange's theorem relate the order of a subgroup to the order of the group. The notion extends to other algebraic structures: subrings, submodules, etc. (Remark 1.14).

# Examples

**Example 1** (p. 11, 1.12): The centre Z(G) = {g in G | gx = xg for all x in G} is a subgroup of G.

**Example 2** (p. 11): (N, +) is closed under addition in (Z, +) but is not a subgroup because it fails (S2): negative integers are not in N.

**Example 3** (p. 12, 1.17): D_n is a subgroup of S_n (the dihedral group as a subgroup of the symmetric group).

# Common Errors

- **Error**: Checking only closure (S1) for an infinite subset and concluding it is a subgroup
  **Correction**: For infinite subsets, (S2) must also be verified; (N, +) subset of (Z, +) is a counterexample

# Common Confusions

- **Confusion**: Thinking any subset closed under the operation is a subgroup
  **Clarification**: The subset must also be nonempty and closed under inverses (though for finite subsets, closure under multiplication suffices)
