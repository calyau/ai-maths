# Quick Definition
The maximal proper submodule $J_\lambda$ of the Verma module $M_\lambda$ is the unique largest proper submodule. It is the sum of all submodules $W \subset M_\lambda$ with $W[\lambda] = 0$, and the irreducible quotient is $L_\lambda = M_\lambda / J_\lambda$.

# Core Definition
(Kirillov, p. 113-114): In the proof of Theorem 9.13, $J_\lambda$ is defined as the sum of all submodules $W \subset M_\lambda$ such that $W[\lambda] = 0$. Every proper submodule has this property (since $W[\lambda] = M_\lambda[\lambda]$ would force $W = M_\lambda$). Thus $J_\lambda$ contains every proper submodule and is itself proper (since $J_\lambda[\lambda] = 0$).

# Prerequisites
- **Verma module** -- $J_\lambda$ is a submodule of $M_\lambda$

# Key Properties
1. $J_\lambda$ is the unique maximal proper submodule of $M_\lambda$
2. $J_\lambda[\lambda] = 0$ (does not contain the highest weight space)
3. $J_\lambda$ contains every other proper submodule of $M_\lambda$
4. $L_\lambda = M_\lambda / J_\lambda$ is the unique irreducible quotient
5. $J_\lambda = 0$ iff $M_\lambda$ is irreducible

# Context & Application
The existence of a unique maximal proper submodule is what guarantees the uniqueness of the irreducible highest-weight module $L_\lambda$. This is a special property of highest-weight modules; general modules need not have a unique maximal proper submodule.

# Examples
**Example** (p. 114): For $\mathfrak{sl}(2, \mathbb{C})$ with $\lambda = n \in \mathbb{Z}_+$, $J_n$ is generated by $f^{n+1}v_\lambda$ and $L_n$ is the $(n+1)$-dimensional irreducible.
