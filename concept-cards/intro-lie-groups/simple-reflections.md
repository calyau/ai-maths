# Quick Definition
Simple reflections are the reflections $s_i = s_{\alpha_i}$ corresponding to simple roots. They generate the Weyl group, and every root is in the $W$-orbit of some simple root under these generators.

# Core Definition
The simple reflections are $s_i = s_{\alpha_i}$ for $\alpha_i \in \Pi$ (Theorem 8.30, p. 100). Theorem 8.30 states:

1. The simple reflections $s_1,\ldots,s_r$ generate $W$.
2. $W(\Pi) = R$: every root $\alpha \in R$ can be written as $w(\alpha_i)$ for some $w \in W$, $\alpha_i \in \Pi$.

# Key Properties
1. $s_i$ generates the Weyl group $W$ (Theorem 8.30(1))
2. $R = W(\Pi)$: every root is a Weyl image of a simple root (Theorem 8.30(2))
3. $l(s_i) = 1$ (Example 8.35, p. 101)
4. $s_i(\alpha_i) = -\alpha_i$ and $s_i$ permutes $R_+ \setminus \{\alpha_i\}$ (Example 8.35)
5. The root system $R$ can be recovered from $\Pi$ via: generate $W$ from $s_i$, then $R = W(\Pi)$ (Corollary 8.33)

# Construction / Recognition
Given simple roots $\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_r$, define $s_i(\lambda) = \lambda - \langle\lambda,\alpha_i^\vee\rangle\alpha_i$ for $i = 1,\ldots,r$.

# Context & Application
Simple reflections are the canonical generators of $W$. They reduce the study of the entire Weyl group to a finite set of generators with explicit relations (Coxeter relations). The fact that $W$ is generated by $r$ reflections (rather than $|R_+|$ reflections) is crucial for computations.

# Examples
**Example 8.32** (p. 101): For $A_{n-1}$, $W = S_n$ and simple reflections are adjacent transpositions $s_i = (i\; i{+}1)$, which are well known to generate $S_n$.

# Common Errors
- **Error**: Thinking every reflection $s_\alpha$ is a simple reflection
  **Correction**: Only $r$ of the reflections (those for simple roots) are simple; others are conjugates
