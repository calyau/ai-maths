# Quick Definition
The Bernstein-Gelfand-Gelfand (BGG) resolution is an exact sequence that resolves the irreducible module $L_\lambda$ in terms of Verma modules indexed by elements of the Weyl group. It provides a powerful tool for computing characters and cohomology.

# Core Definition
(Kirillov, pp. 116): Section 9.4 studies the structure of the submodules $M_i \subset M_\lambda$ constructed in the proof of Theorem 9.17. Lemma 9.20 shows that submodules generated by singular vectors (vectors $v \in M_\lambda[\mu]$ with $e_\alpha v = 0$ for all $\alpha \in R_+$) are themselves Verma modules.

The BGG resolution takes the form of an exact sequence:
$$0 \to M_{w_0 \cdot \lambda} \to \cdots \to \bigoplus_{\ell(w) = k} M_{w \cdot \lambda} \to \cdots \to M_\lambda \to L_\lambda \to 0$$

where $w \cdot \lambda = w(\lambda + \rho) - \rho$ is the shifted Weyl group action.

# Prerequisites
- **Verma module** -- the terms in the resolution
- **Irreducible highest-weight module** -- the module being resolved
- **Weyl group** -- indexes the Verma modules in the resolution

# Key Properties
1. Each term in the resolution is a direct sum of Verma modules
2. The Verma modules are indexed by Weyl group elements, graded by length
3. Submodules of Verma modules generated by singular vectors are themselves Verma modules (Lemma 9.20)
4. The proof that submodules are Verma uses the no-zero-divisors property of $U\mathfrak{n}_-$ (Corollary 4.64)

# Context & Application
The BGG resolution is one of the deepest results in the representation theory of semisimple Lie algebras. It provides a "free resolution" of $L_\lambda$ in the category of highest-weight modules. Taking Euler characteristics of this resolution yields the Weyl character formula.

# Examples
**Example** (p. 116): For $\mathfrak{sl}(2, \mathbb{C})$ with $\lambda = n \in \mathbb{Z}_+$, the BGG resolution is the short exact sequence $0 \to M_{-n-2} \to M_n \to L_n \to 0$, reflecting the fact that $J_n$ is generated by $f^{n+1}v_\lambda$ which gives a Verma submodule $M_{s \cdot n} = M_{-n-2}$.

# Common Confusions
- **Confusion**: Thinking the BGG resolution is a resolution by irreducible modules
  **Clarification**: It is a resolution by Verma modules, which are typically reducible and infinite-dimensional
