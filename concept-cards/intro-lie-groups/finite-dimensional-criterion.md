# Quick Definition
The irreducible highest-weight module $L_\lambda$ is finite-dimensional if and only if $\lambda$ is a dominant integral weight ($\lambda \in P_+$). This theorem provides the complete classification of irreducible finite-dimensional representations.

# Core Definition
**Theorem 9.17** (Kirillov, p. 114): Irreducible highest-weight representation $L_\lambda$ is finite-dimensional iff $\lambda \in P_+$.

**Corollary 9.18** (p. 114): For every $\lambda \in P_+$, $L_\lambda$ is an irreducible finite-dimensional representation. These representations are pairwise non-isomorphic, and every irreducible finite-dimensional representation is isomorphic to one of them.

# Prerequisites
- **Irreducible highest-weight module** -- the modules being classified
- **Dominant integral weight** -- the condition for finite-dimensionality
- **Verma module** -- used in the proof construction

# Key Properties
1. Necessity ($L_\lambda$ finite-dimensional $\Rightarrow$ $\lambda \in P_+$): follows from restricting to $\mathfrak{sl}(2, \mathbb{C})$ subalgebras
2. Sufficiency ($\lambda \in P_+ \Rightarrow$ $L_\lambda$ finite-dimensional): uses the construction of $\tilde{L}_\lambda = M_\lambda / \sum M_i$ (Theorem 9.19)
3. The proof constructs singular vectors $v_{s_i \cdot \lambda} = f_i^{n_i+1} v_\lambda$ for each simple root (equation (9.9))
4. The representations $\{L_\lambda \mid \lambda \in P_+\}$ are pairwise non-isomorphic

# Construction / Recognition
The proof of sufficiency proceeds in three steps:
1. **Step 1**: For each simple root $\alpha_i$, construct the vector $v_{s_i \cdot \lambda} = f_i^{n_i+1} v_\lambda$ where $n_i = \langle \alpha_i^\vee, \lambda \rangle$, and show $e_j v_{s_i \cdot \lambda} = 0$ for all $j$
2. **Step 2**: Let $M_i$ be the submodule generated by $v_{s_i \cdot \lambda}$; form $\tilde{L}_\lambda = M_\lambda / \sum M_i$
3. **Step 3**: Prove $\tilde{L}_\lambda$ is finite-dimensional (Theorem 9.19); then $L_\lambda$ is a quotient of $\tilde{L}_\lambda$, hence also finite-dimensional

# Context & Application
This is the culminating classification theorem of Chapter 9. Together with complete reducibility (Section 6.9), it gives a complete understanding of the representation theory: every finite-dimensional representation is a direct sum of $L_\lambda$'s with $\lambda \in P_+$, and these are completely determined by their highest weight.

# Examples
**Example** (p. 114): For $\mathfrak{sl}(2, \mathbb{C})$, $P_+ = \mathbb{Z}_+$ and $L_n$ is the $(n+1)$-dimensional irreducible module (Section 5.1).

# Common Errors
- **Error**: Assuming the theorem provides an explicit construction of $L_\lambda$
  **Correction**: The theorem proves existence and uniqueness but does not give an explicit basis; for classical algebras, explicit constructions exist (e.g., Section 9.6 for $\mathfrak{sl}(n, \mathbb{C})$)
