# Quick Definition
A singular vector in a Verma module $M_\lambda$ is a weight vector $v \in M_\lambda[\mu]$ (with $\mu \neq \lambda$) that is annihilated by all positive root vectors: $e_\alpha v = 0$ for all $\alpha \in R_+$. Such vectors generate Verma submodules.

# Core Definition
(Kirillov, pp. 114-116): In the proof of Theorem 9.17, the vectors $v_{s_i \cdot \lambda} = f_i^{n_i + 1} v_\lambda$ (where $n_i = \langle \alpha_i^\vee, \lambda \rangle$) are singular vectors satisfying $e_j v_{s_i \cdot \lambda} = 0$ for all $j$.

**Lemma 9.20** (p. 116): If $v \in M_\lambda[\mu]$ satisfies $e_\alpha v = 0$ for all $\alpha \in R_+$, then the submodule generated by $v$ is isomorphic to the Verma module $M_\mu$.

# Prerequisites
- **Verma module** -- singular vectors live inside Verma modules
- **Highest-weight vector** -- a singular vector is a highest-weight vector of a submodule

# Key Properties
1. Singular vectors generate Verma submodules (Lemma 9.20)
2. The key singular vectors in the proof of Theorem 9.17 are $f_i^{n_i+1} v_\lambda$ with weight $s_i \cdot \lambda = \lambda - (n_i + 1)\alpha_i$
3. The submodule $M_i$ generated by $v_{s_i \cdot \lambda}$ is proper (since $\lambda$ is not a weight of $M_i$)
4. The proof that $e_j v_{s_i \cdot \lambda} = 0$ for $j \neq i$ uses $[e_j, f_i] = 0$; for $j = i$ it uses $\mathfrak{sl}(2, \mathbb{C})$ theory

# Context & Application
Singular vectors are the key to understanding the submodule structure of Verma modules. The maximal proper submodule $J_\lambda$ is generated by singular vectors. The BGG resolution is built from the pattern of Verma submodules, which correspond to singular vectors at weights $w \cdot \lambda$ for $w \in W$.

# Examples
**Example** (p. 114-115): For $\lambda \in P_+$, with $n_i = \langle \alpha_i^\vee, \lambda \rangle$, the vector $f_i^{n_i+1} v_\lambda \in M_\lambda[s_i \cdot \lambda]$ is singular. It generates a Verma submodule $M_i \cong M_{s_i \cdot \lambda}$.
