# Quick Definition
A highest-weight representation is a non-zero representation generated by a single vector $v$ of weight $\lambda$ that is annihilated by all positive root vectors. The weight $\lambda$ is the highest weight, and $v$ is the highest-weight vector.

# Core Definition
**Definition 9.4** (Kirillov, p. 111): A non-zero representation $V$ (not necessarily finite-dimensional) of $\mathfrak{g}$ is called a *highest weight representation* if it is generated by a vector $v \in V[\lambda]$ such that $e_\alpha \cdot v = 0$ for all $\alpha \in R_+$. The vector $v$ is called the *highest weight vector*, and $\lambda$ is the *highest weight* of $V$.

# Prerequisites
- **Weight of a representation** -- the highest weight is a specific weight
- **Weight decomposition theorem** -- ensures weight spaces exist
- **Root action on weights** -- explains why the generating vector must be annihilated by positive root vectors
- **Borel subalgebra** -- the annihilation condition involves $\mathfrak{n}_+$

# Key Properties
1. The highest weight $\lambda$ is unique (Corollary 9.12)
2. The highest-weight vector is unique up to scalar (Corollary 9.12)
3. All weights satisfy $\mu \in \lambda - Q_+$ (Theorem 9.10)
4. $\dim V[\lambda] = 1$ (Theorem 9.10(3))
5. $V$ may be infinite-dimensional
6. Every highest-weight representation is a quotient of the Verma module $M_\lambda$ (Lemma 9.8)

# Construction / Recognition
## To recognize a highest-weight representation
1. Find a weight $\lambda$ such that $\lambda + \alpha \notin P(V)$ for all $\alpha \in R_+$
2. Verify that $V$ is generated by a non-zero vector in $V[\lambda]$
3. Equivalently, check that the vector is annihilated by all of $\mathfrak{n}_+$ and generates $V$ under $U\mathfrak{g}$

## To construct one
1. Start with a one-dimensional representation $\mathbb{C}_\lambda$ of the Borel subalgebra $\mathfrak{b} = \mathfrak{h} \oplus \mathfrak{n}_+$
2. Induce to get the Verma module: $M_\lambda = U\mathfrak{g} \otimes_{U\mathfrak{b}} \mathbb{C}_\lambda$
3. Take any quotient of $M_\lambda$

# Context & Application
**Theorem 9.5** (p. 111): Every irreducible finite-dimensional representation of $\mathfrak{g}$ is a highest-weight representation. This is the key theorem that reduces the classification problem to studying highest-weight modules. The proof constructs the highest weight as a weight $\lambda$ maximal in the sense that $\lambda + \alpha \notin P(V)$ for all $\alpha \in R_+$.

# Examples
**Example** (p. 112): For $\mathfrak{g} = \mathfrak{sl}(2, \mathbb{C})$, a highest-weight representation with highest weight $\lambda$ is generated by a vector $v$ satisfying $ev = 0$ and $hv = \lambda v$. The finite-dimensional ones have $\lambda \in \mathbb{Z}_+$.

# Common Errors
- **Error**: Assuming every highest-weight representation is irreducible
  **Correction**: Verma modules are highest-weight representations that are typically reducible

# Common Confusions
- **Confusion**: Thinking "highest weight" means the weight with the largest norm
  **Clarification**: "Highest" means maximal with respect to the partial order defined by positive roots: no positive root can be added to it while staying in $P(V)$
