# Quick Definition
The Weyl group $W$ of a root system $R$ is the finite subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}(E)$ generated by all reflections $s_\alpha$, $\alpha \in R$. It is a finite subgroup of the orthogonal group $O(E)$ that permutes the roots.

# Core Definition
The Weyl group $W$ of a root system $R$ is the subgroup of $\mathrm{GL}(E)$ generated by reflections $s_\alpha$, $\alpha \in R$ (Definition 8.6, p. 92).

By Lemma 8.7: (1) $W$ is a finite subgroup of $O(E)$, and $R$ is invariant under $W$. (2) For any $w \in W$, $s_{w(\alpha)} = w\,s_\alpha\,w^{-1}$.

# Key Properties
1. $W$ is finite (since it embeds into $\mathrm{Aut}(R)$, and $R$ is a finite set)
2. $W \subset O(E)$ (each generator $s_\alpha$ is orthogonal)
3. $w(R) = R$ for all $w \in W$ (roots are permuted by $W$)
4. Conjugation: $s_{w(\alpha)} = w\,s_\alpha\,w^{-1}$ (Lemma 8.7)
5. $W$ acts simply transitively on Weyl chambers (Corollary 8.38, p. 102)
6. $W$ is generated by simple reflections alone (Theorem 8.30)
7. Not every automorphism of a root system is in $W$ (e.g., $\alpha \mapsto -\alpha$ for $A_n$, $n > 2$)

# Construction / Recognition
## To compute the Weyl group
1. Choose a set of simple roots $\Pi = \{\alpha_1,\ldots,\alpha_r\}$
2. The simple reflections $s_i = s_{\alpha_i}$ generate $W$ (Theorem 8.30)
3. Determine the group presented by these generators subject to the Coxeter relations: $s_i^2 = 1$, $(s_i s_j)^{m_{ij}} = 1$ (Exercise 8.8)

# Context & Application
The Weyl group is central to the entire theory. It acts on roots, weights, and Weyl chambers. Its transitive action on Weyl chambers (Theorem 8.25) shows that different choices of polarization yield equivalent sets of simple roots. In representation theory, the Weyl group controls the symmetry of weight diagrams and appears in the Weyl character formula.

# Examples
**Example 8.8** (p. 92): For the root system $A_{n-1}$, $W = S_n$ (the symmetric group). The reflections $s_{e_i - e_j}$ are transpositions, and they generate $S_n$.

**Example** (p. 92): For $A_1$, $W = S_2 = \mathbb{Z}_2 = \{1,\sigma\}$ where $\sigma$ acts by $\lambda \mapsto -\lambda$.

# Common Errors
- **Error**: Assuming every root system automorphism belongs to the Weyl group
  **Correction**: The map $\alpha \mapsto -\alpha$ is an automorphism of $A_n$ for $n > 2$ but is not in $W$ (p. 92)

# Common Confusions
- **Confusion**: Thinking the Weyl group depends on the choice of polarization
  **Clarification**: $W$ is generated by all reflections $s_\alpha$, $\alpha \in R$, not just simple reflections; the polarization is not needed to define $W$, though it identifies convenient generators
