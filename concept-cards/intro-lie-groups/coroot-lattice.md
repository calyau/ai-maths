# Quick Definition
The coroot lattice $Q^\vee$ is the abelian group generated by all coroots $\alpha^\vee$, $\alpha \in R$. It equals $\bigoplus \mathbb{Z}\alpha_i^\vee$ and lives in $E^*$. The weight lattice $P$ is its dual lattice.

# Core Definition
The coroot lattice is defined by (equation 8.9, p. 96):

$$Q^\vee = \{\text{abelian group generated by } \alpha^\vee,\, \alpha \in R\} \subset E^*$$

By Exercise 8.2, $Q^\vee = \bigoplus \mathbb{Z}\alpha_i^\vee$ (equation 8.11, p. 97).

# Prerequisites
- **coroot** -- the elements generating $Q^\vee$
- **simple-roots** -- the simple coroots $\alpha_i^\vee$ form a basis

# Key Properties
1. $Q^\vee = \bigoplus_{i=1}^r \mathbb{Z}\alpha_i^\vee$
2. The weight lattice $P$ is exactly the dual lattice of $Q^\vee$ (p. 97)
3. $Q^\vee$ has rank $r$

# Construction / Recognition
1. Compute simple coroots $\alpha_i^\vee$ from simple roots
2. $Q^\vee = \mathbb{Z}\alpha_1^\vee \oplus \cdots \oplus \mathbb{Z}\alpha_r^\vee$

# Context & Application
The coroot lattice is the "measuring stick" for the weight lattice: $P$ consists precisely of those $\lambda \in E$ for which $\langle\lambda,\alpha^\vee\rangle \in \mathbb{Z}$ for all $\alpha^\vee \in Q^\vee$. In representation theory, the coroot lattice determines which weights are integral.

# Examples
**Example 8.20** (p. 97): For $A_1$ with $(\alpha,\alpha) = 2$, $\alpha^\vee = \alpha$ under $E^* \simeq E$, so $Q^\vee \cong Q = \mathbb{Z}\alpha$.
